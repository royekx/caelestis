The Helm

A spelljammer helm is a magically prepared seat — in most configurations a throne or large chair — that allows a spellcasting occupant to propel a vessel through Wildspace. Attunement to the helm is near-immediate for a qualified caster. Once attuned, the helmsperson's will becomes the ship's propulsion. The vessel moves, turns, accelerates, and stops in response to the helmsperson's directed intent. Experienced pilots report that the connection, at its most refined, feels less like operating machinery and more like extended sensation — the hull as a skin, the void as surrounding space.

Attunement is exclusive and total. While seated and attuned to a functioning helm, the spelljammer cannot cast other spells. The helm draws on the same magical capacity that would otherwise power spellcasting. This is not a malfunction; it is the design. Plan crew assignments and operational roles accordingly. A vessel with a single qualified spelljammer is a vessel whose mage will not be available for combat, support, or any other arcane function during their shift.

Speed and manoeuvrability scale with the caster's ability. A high-level caster produces a faster, more responsive ship. A less experienced one produces a slower, less manoeuvrable vessel — still spaceworthy, but limited. This matters most in combat and in time-sensitive approaches. Know what your helmsperson can do before you need to depend on it.

Vessel Types in Common Use

The following are the vessel types most commonly encountered in Realmspace operations and the ones most likely to appear in training and early Fleet assignments. Additional vessel types are documented in supplementary Fleet intelligence as cadets progress through operational postings.

Hammerhead

The Fleet's standard mid-sized combat and multipurpose vessel. Compact, durable, well-armed for its size, and forward-heavy in its armament configuration. The hammerhead's main weapons bear forward, making it effective in pursuit and direct engagement. It is the primary training vessel at Caelestis and the platform against which most cadet exercises are designed. If you are going to know one ship well, know this one.

Squid Ship

Fast, light, and agile. The squid ship's elongated hull and aft appendages allow it to move and change heading more quickly than heavier vessels. Favoured by pirates, independent captains, and crews that prioritise speed over armament. It carries less crew and fewer weapons than the hammerhead. In a direct fight with a hammerhead, the squid ship is outgunned. In a chase, the outcome depends on who is at the helm.

Galleon

A large cargo platform adapted for Wildspace. Slow, heavily loaded, and not designed for combat or manoeuvre. The galley feeds a large crew; the holds move significant cargo. Galleons serve the logistical backbone of any extended operation — resupply, transport, and the movement of materials the Fleet cannot do without. They are not combat ships and should not be treated as such.

Crew Roles

Spelljamming crew structure follows a naval pattern modified for the demands of void operation. The critical addition that has no direct surface equivalent is the spelljammer — the caster who operates the helm. Everything else flows from conventional naval hierarchy: captain, boatswain, navigator, gunners, and crew.

In practice, small crew vessels require individuals to cover multiple roles simultaneously. The navigator may be the helmsperson. The boatswain may double as a gunner. On a vessel with four or five crew, each person is performing the work of two or three positions by surface naval standards. Operational flexibility is not optional. Assign roles, but train for every role your vessel requires.
