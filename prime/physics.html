Layer 01·Cosmology·The Physics

Physics

What is true. The structural rules underneath every world, every faction, every player choice. The Lore Bible says what is real; the Campaign layer says what matters; the World layer says where it lives. This is the bedrock.

Core Premise

Caelestis begins with Spelljammer Academy and expands into a wider multiversal conflict. At the surface level, this is a Spelljammer campaign about cadets, crews, exploration, dangerous missions, Wildspace politics, and escalating threats beyond Toril. Beneath that surface, the campaign is about structural pressure within reality itself, how worlds approach metaphysical thresholds, and how Vecna seeks to exploit those thresholds to alter the Prime Continuum.

The hidden architecture of the campaign is built around pressure, not random cosmic spectacle. Worlds rise toward Zeniths. Monoliths appear in response to those conditions. Factions intervene with incomplete knowledge and conflicting priorities. Vecna is the first major antagonist in the setting to understand enough of that architecture to weaponize it at scale.

The campaign should feel layered. Early adventures can function as military training, transport, reconnaissance, rescue, diplomacy, exploration, or monster-hunting. Over time, those missions should reveal a consistent pattern: reality is under strain, several organizations are trying to manage that strain, and Vecna is not simply trying to conquer worlds — he is trying to rewrite what it means for anything to exist at all.

The Prime Continuum

The multiverse in this campaign is called the Prime Continuum. It is not simply a collection of worlds floating beside one another. It is a structured totality governed by deeper principles that precede worlds, gods, civilizations, and histories. Planes, worlds, souls, magic systems, histories, afterlives, and cosmic cycles all exist inside it. The Prime Continuum is the full field of reality.

The Prime Continuum remains stable only so long as its foundational axioms remain in dynamic tension with one another. This is not a static balance. It is an active equilibrium. Pressure, motion, contradiction, escalation, convergence, conflict, creation, and destruction all feed the condition from which reality persists.

The Wheel and Spokes Model

The simplest way to think about the Continuum is as a wheel. The four foundational axioms are the spokes. Continuity is the integrity of the wheel as a whole. Continuity is not simply a fifth equal piece — it is the emergent condition created by the other four remaining in relation.

If one axiom is destroyed, displaced, overwritten, or replaced, Continuity changes with it. That change does not remain isolated. It cascades downward through reality. Worlds, magic, metaphysical laws, identities, afterlives, histories, and even definitions of personhood begin to shift until a new equilibrium is forced into place.

Player-facing echo "You are trying to convince reality to accept a new condition. And reality, under sufficient pressure, has a way of listening." — already planted in the FIA. The line that surfaces the Continuum at player scale without naming it.

The Four Axioms

The campaign currently rests on four foundational axioms. Two are fully locked in canon: Existence and Convergence. The remaining two are functionally locked even if their final ceremonial names remain flexible.

Existence Locked Active thread

Existence is the axiom that reality is. It governs being, presence, ontology, persistence, and the distinction between what is real and what is not. It includes more than life. It includes souls, matter, worlds, constructs, memory, identity, history, and even absences defined against what should be present.

Existence is the axiom Vecna is best positioned to target. This aligns with his nature. Vecna does not simply crave rulership, worship, or immortality. He seeks indispensability. To replace or corrupt Existence is to force all reality to pass through him as a condition of being.

Existence is creation and destruction both

Of the four axioms, Existence is the one currently live in play — because it is Vecna's target and because three PCs (Gregory, Boogie, and arguably Sol) are already entangled with it.

Existence is not only being — it is being and its absence, creation and destruction. The breadth above is the key. Existence is not the "good" axiom. It is the axiom of whether a thing is at all — which necessarily includes the unmaking of things. This produces a spectrum the campaign can mine:

Anomalies — the generative face
Things the Axiom touched on its generative side. They are more than they should be. Gregory is one. The Latchling is another. They are rare, individual, and they did not choose it.
The destructive face
Things the Axiom touched on its unmaking side, or things that embody Existence-as-erasure. The clockwork horrors sit here: made things that consume made things to make more of themselves, with no concept that the consumed were ever someone. They are the exact inverse of Gregory's question. Where Gregory asks "is a made thing a person?", the horrors are reality answering no, it's inventory.
The in-between
Left deliberately open. The horrors may be more than mindless; they may sit between the axioms in a way the crew can investigate "one day." Do not resolve this. It is a held question.
Player knowledge Players never learn the word "Axiom." It's cosmic-unknowable (big-bang-tier). They learn that specific things are wrong or changed. Theories are for cosmologists; no one in this crew is on that path.
Convergence Locked

Convergence governs the drawing-together of systems, powers, meanings, and destinies. It is the principle by which isolated things become linked, by which threads form networks, and by which separate pressures collide into one recognizable pattern.

Monolith responses, Zenith clustering, prophetic overlap, and synchronized world-level crises all express Convergence in some form.

In play Convergence is why the network's spectrum of factions can be a real pattern without coordination. They don't have to plan together — they reach for the same power from different directions, and Convergence draws their efforts toward the same pressure points. See Campaign → The Network.
Intensification & Escalation Functional

This axiom governs the tendency of forces to intensify. Power centralizes. Pressure mounts. Conditions sharpen. Small tensions rarely remain small forever. They gather momentum until they become crises, breakthroughs, collapses, or transformations.

This is one reason Zeniths occur at all. Worlds do not drift gently into perfect equilibrium. Civilizations concentrate power. Wars escalate. Rituals amplify. Magical systems reach saturation. Individuals and cultures cross thresholds. The Continuum naturally produces intensification.

Conflict & Friction Functional

This axiom governs contest, opposition, contradiction, resistance, and transformative struggle. Reality is not built from harmony alone. It is shaped through collision: ideals against ideals, powers against powers, forms against dissolution, wills against circumstance.

This is broader than warfare. It includes metaphysical incompatibility, ideological contradiction, ecological imbalance, divine rivalry, personal fracture, and the push-and-pull through which identity and history are formed.

Continuity Emergent

Continuity is the emergent function created by the four axioms remaining in dynamic relation. It is not fully sentient in the ordinary sense, but neither is it inert. It expresses itself through systems, pressures, persistent patterns, and corrective responses. Continuity does not micromanage history. It preserves the possibility of history continuing.

Continuity is why the multiverse does not collapse every time a world nears catastrophe. It is also why the multiverse produces responses such as monoliths. These responses are not moral judgments. They are structural answers to metaphysical pressure.

Zeniths

A Zenith is a metaphysical apex: a point at which a world, culture, magical system, conflict, or convergence of forces reaches extreme significance. Zeniths are threshold events. They are not inherently apocalyptic. Some become disasters. Some become renewals. Some become coronations, revelations, collapses, or irreversible turnings.

A Zenith can arise naturally through history, or it can be forced through mortal action. Mortals seeking power often produce the most unstable Zeniths because they try to compress destiny into ritual, conquest, perfection, or control. This does not make mortals uniquely evil. It makes them the actors most likely to force a world past safe thresholds.

The inscription, in campaign terms A Zenith is the act of inscribing a new glyph — forcing a place past its threshold writes a new mark where there wasn't one. This is the mechanism Vecna's working depends on. See Campaign → The Inscription.

Monoliths

Monoliths (also called Ortholiths) are an emergent response of Continuity. They are not gods. They are not divine gifts. They are not omens sent by celestial personalities. They appear because the structure of the Continuum produces them under specific conditions.

Monoliths appear at Zeniths. When they first emerge, they are blank or unreadable. They do not arrive with a moral message or fixed agenda. They absorb resonant energies from the event, culture, magical structure, or historical pressure around them. In effect, they become archives of whatever has reached peak significance.

This archival function is central. Monoliths do not cause apocalypses. They do not arrive in order to destroy worlds. They appear because something in a world has become significant enough that reality begins preserving it. In destructive cases, the same forces that produce the Zenith often make the surrounding event apocalyptic. The monolith is a response to that condition, not its cause.

Monoliths typically persist until the triggering conditions of the Zenith resolve. If a world is saved, the monolith may complete its archival role and eventually return to the greater system. If the Zenith ends in catastrophe, the monolith still archives what it can. If the monolith is corrupted or destroyed before that process completes, the damage is severe. In extreme cases, a world or civilization can be lost not only physically, but to the universe's own memory.

Corrupted Monoliths

A monolith can be corrupted by outside influence. This does not change its origin, but it distorts its function. A corrupted monolith may archive incompletely, distort what it preserves, magnify destructive resonance, or become a vector for hostile metaphysical influence.

This is especially dangerous because many factions wrongly assume monoliths are either sacred relics or evil artifacts. In truth, they are structural instruments. Mishandling them out of fear or reverence can be equally catastrophic.

The inscription, in campaign terms A monolith is a glyph already written — a place where the inscription exists in reality. Vecna's working needs specific glyphs in specific places: claim the standing ones, force Zeniths to inscribe the missing ones. See Campaign → The Inscription.

The Core, the Radiant Citadel, and the All-Father

The Core and the Radiant Citadel

The Core Monolith is the origin point, sink, archive, and stabilizing nexus of the broader monolith system. It is the ark of the Continuum. All monoliths ultimately relate back to it.

The Radiant Citadel accreted around the Core over uncountable time — drawn together by the same archival pressure that produces monoliths in the first place. The Citadel is what reached the surface of awareness; the Core itself is hidden from corporeal beings, layered beneath. Most travelers who encounter the Citadel understand only a surface truth: a place of refuge, culture, transit, and connection. Beneath that role lies its deeper function as the multiverse's great archival and stabilizing mechanism.

Correction from prior canon The Citadel is not close to Caelestis. It is hidden away — its exact relationship to mortal space is itself a late-arc reveal. Caelestis is a strategic node on its own merits, not a Citadel satellite. Where prior notes implied proximity, treat it as discarded.

The Core is not public knowledge. The title Mother of the Universe belongs instead to hidden doctrine associated with the Restorers. They believe the Core can be reached, understood, and perhaps used to restore worlds, civilizations, and histories lost to catastrophe, archival failure, or metaphysical erasure.

Most gods do not understand the Core. Most mortal factions do not know it exists in its true form. Even those who encounter the Radiant Citadel rarely glimpse what it sits atop.

The All-Father

The All-Father is not a primordial creator god in the conventional sense. The All-Father is the Core made sentient through accumulation, pressure, and recursive significance. Over time, as monoliths returned resonance and meaning to the Core, a persona emerged from the function.

The All-Father is real, but it is best understood as the personification of a cosmic function rather than a god standing outside creation. The All-Father rarely acts directly. Its influence is diffuse, often felt through the Order, the Watchers, or those unusually sensitive to Continuity and Zenith pressure.

Paracausality

Paracausal = the reality of a thing has been changed. Not whether it exists — how it exists. The term is borrowed (Destiny); it fits because it means operating outside normal cause-and-effect. Paracausality does not mean "made magical."

Paracausality and the Weave are orthogonal concerns. Paracausality describes what changed. The Weave governs why anti-magic does or doesn't suppress. Keep them separate.

Paracausality vs. the Weave

The Weave is the fabric mortals reach into to cast spells. Anti-magic severs local Weave access — it doesn't delete energy. The consequences fall out cleanly:

  • Weave-acts (spellcasting) are suppressed inside anti-magic.
  • A spell slot is innate stored energy. It persists inside anti-magic — it just can't be cast.
  • Innate, non-Weave processes (flowers' anti-magic emission, wisps' draining feed, the counter-spore plant's spore-conversion) are not suppressed. This also dissolves the "flowers cancel their own anti-magic" paradox.
Plain-language analogy for players Energy is underground water — the same for all. The Weave is the well mortals use to reach it. Anti-magic caps the well. The plant has roots — a non-Weave path to the same water — but it's a conduit with no reserves, so it needs a caster to supply a spell slot. Caster has the water; plant has the roots. Answers both "why the plant and not me?" and "why my slot?"

Counter-spore plant specification

  • Fuel with a spell slot (non-Weave conduit — works in anti-magic field).
  • Bonus action to activate.
  • The slot is the cost; slot level scales area and duration.
  • At L3, caps at 2nd-level slot (~two rungs of scaling).
  • Innate obscurity-clear — faerie-fire-like, not a cast spell.
  • Caster-only.

Forward hook: this is a category, not a one-off. Future flora-finds are a scalable non-Weave reward vein.

Paracausal entities in play

Paracausal change is rare and individual. Each known case in the campaign:

Gregory — the ability to wield magic
Autognomes are constructs. No other autognome in this world can cast. That is Gregory's paracausality and his self-surfacing tell. The resonance (Calibration, Bearing) is a consequence of the change, not the definition of it.
Mr. Blip — the control case
Personhood and soul are within normal autognome range — Blip has both. Magic is the line. Blip can't cast; Gregory can. If players wonder "is Blip like Gregory?" — no. Blip is the proof that ordinary autognomes have selves; Gregory's anomaly is the magic, not the personhood.
Wizpop surfaces it
An ordinary autognome reacting with wonder, not alarm, to Gregory casting: "You cast that — we don't — what are you?" Surfaces the difference through a peer; plants the question in Gregory's own head, gently.
The Latchling — sentience
A paracausal change of a different shape. Its reality has been altered along a different axis: it has become aware where its kind are not.

Canon Guardrails

The following points remain fixed unless intentionally revised. When the table presses on a thing, these are the answers reality has already chosen.

Cosmology
  • Monoliths do not cause Zeniths. They respond to them.
  • Zeniths are not always apocalyptic.
  • Mortal-made Zeniths are often the most dangerous because they are forced.
  • Destroying a monolith is not a clean solution. It risks archival failure and deeper loss.
  • The gods do not have full mastery of the Prime Continuum.
Factions
  • The Order of the All-Father knows more than most factions, but still operates with incomplete understanding.
  • Watchers are a known, respected partner organization — not ominous, not omnipotent world-police. The Fleet works with them routinely. Their internals are discreet; their existence is not secret.
  • Restorers are a hidden, independent faction pursuing the Mother of the Universe for their own restorative agenda. They are not Watcher partners or ordinary party allies.
Vecna
  • Vecna's plan is systemic, not local. Local incidents matter because they are components.
  • Vecna's advantage is comprehension weaponized — not raw power alone.

Flexible Areas

Deliberately left broad so the campaign can adapt without contradiction:

  • The final ceremonial names of the two functional but not fully formalized axioms beyond Existence and Convergence.
  • The exact internal politics of the Bridge.
  • The full roster and hierarchy of Watchers and Restorers.
  • Which worlds or modules become central in the sandbox arc.
  • How much the public, the gods, or individual allied leaders know at any given moment.
  • Which monolith events are natural and which were manipulated.

Use at the Table

When introducing a new location, crisis, faction, or artifact, ask:

  1. Is this event building toward, reacting to, or recovering from a threshold?
  2. Which faction notices it first, and why?
  3. Does the event intensify, converge, escalate, or fracture existing pressures?
  4. Would Vecna benefit if everyone misread this as a local problem?
  5. What would a Watcher do here?
  6. If a hidden Restorer interest exists here, what would it want?
  7. What part of reality is actually at stake beneath the surface story?

If those questions are answerable, the campaign remains aligned with its own cosmology even when the players take the story in unexpected directions.

Core Truth

Caelestis is a Spelljammer campaign built on the idea that exploration and cosmic wonder sit directly beside structural peril. The stars are beautiful, but beauty does not imply safety. Worlds are worth saving, but not every threat is obvious at first glance. Institutions can protect reality without understanding it fully. Vecna is dangerous because he understands just enough of the Prime Continuum to try to become part of its foundation.

That is the campaign's core truth at the level of physics. For what makes that matter — for the human question underneath the cosmic one — see Campaign → The North.
