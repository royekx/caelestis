Campaign

What matters. The story this campaign is telling, the villain plan it runs against, the architecture of Arc II. Physics says what is true; this says what is for. When in doubt, return to The North.

Who decides what's real?

The North

Who decides what's real? That is the campaign, in five words.

The monoliths, the Zeniths, the network, the heist — all of it is the pressure that forces the question into the open. The question is the thing. "Real" means: counts; persists by right; gets to exist as itself rather than as someone else's function, error, property, or permission slip. The whole campaign is a long argument about who gets to grant that, withhold it, or claim it — and whether anyone has the right to decide it for anyone else.

Why this crew

They are not six adventurers who met in a tavern. They are six people each handed a verdict — told, in some specific way, that they don't fully count — slowly deciding, together, to send those verdicts back.

Gregory — inventory
A made thing asking whether a made thing can be a person.
Sol — error
A man his world declared a mistake, existing through sheer refusal to stop.
Boogie — container
Becoming a vessel for the unmade; does it matter that something survived in him?
Tumak — constructed self
Two people as one, asking whether a self you built is still real or a lie that works.
Casey — instrument
Guided toward a purpose he didn't choose, by a voice he trusts but doesn't understand.
Bartholomew — the thing he won't look at
Circling a shape already inside him, deciding whether to recognize what he already is.

Same question, six keys. That unity is the engine to lean on, not a coincidence to hide.

Why Vecna

Vecna is the negative space of the crew — the answer each of them fears is true, scaled to cosmic and made permanent:

You are real only if something with power says so.

He wants to overwrite Existence so all reality must pass through him. He needs no manifesto; he is the verdict each PC already heard once, in their own voice, on their worst day. The crew doesn't fight him because he's evil. They fight him because they know, personally, exactly what he's selling.

The found family is the mechanism, not the decoration

You become real by being seen as real by people who choose you.

The crew is each other's proof. That's what a found family is here — the literal mechanism of the theme, not a side-warmth. The emotional spine and the thematic spine are the same spine. Pay off the bond and you've paid off the campaign.

Not everything is Vecna — and that's the point

The recurring misread to avoid: "Vecna is behind everything." He isn't. The truth is worse and more useful:

Everyone is doing a small version of what Vecna does big. The corrupt official deciding a person doesn't count. The cult deciding their meaning is worth a world. A Watcher deciding a civilization can't be saved. The Syndicate treating beings as cargo. None of these are Vecna — they're the ordinary face of the same impulse, which is exactly why Vecna can hide inside it. The crew learns to see the cosmic by first learning to see the small.

The mid-tier factions are a spectrum of the same sin — each a different answer to who gets to decide what's real:

FactionTheir answer
ConcordanceWe do — we enforce which reality is legitimate.
ArchivistsWe do — we hoard what's allowed to be remembered.
SyndicateNobody, and that's profitable — beings are cargo.
Fracture CultWe do — we force meaning by breaking the world into a sharper shape.
Rogue WatcherI do — I decide who survives, for their own good.
The crewThe thing itself does. You don't get to decide that for someone.

They don't coordinate. They converge — all reaching for the same power from different directions. The convergence is the plot; the question is why it matters.

The table test

For any situation, NPC, world, mission, or idea, ask one thing:

The test Who here is trying to decide what's real — for themselves or for someone else — and what does it cost?

Answerable → it's a Caelestis story; build it. Not answerable → it's texture, not story; run it light. This is what replaces the module. You never invent "what happens" — you ask who's deciding and who's being decided about, and the scene appears.

The three zoom levels

The whole architecture is one question at three scales:

Cosmic
The network / Vecna's inscription. The question at the scale of all reality.
Local
A world, faction, or mission. The question at the scale of a place and its people.
Personal
Each PC's arc. The question at the scale of one self.

A great session stacks all three in one scene: a PC's private verdict surfaces (personal) inside a local crisis (local) that turns out to be a fingerprint of the cosmic war (cosmic). When those three rhyme in one moment, the campaign is working.

Vecna's Plan — The Inscription

Vecna's working is a cosmic inscription — a spell written into the substance of Existence, glyph by glyph. This is the through-line that makes the whole villain plan legible and thematic at once.

The mechanism

A monolith is a glyph already written
A place where the inscription exists in reality. Standing monoliths are ink already on the page.
A Zenith is the act of inscribing a new glyph
Forcing a place past its threshold writes a new mark where there wasn't one. This is why Zeniths matter to him: each forced threshold is a stroke of the pen.
The working requires specific glyphs in specific places
Where a monolith already stands, Vecna must locate and claim it. Where one is needed but absent, he must inscribe it — by forcing a Zenith. That is what "activating a node" means: claim an existing glyph, or manufacture a new one through a forced threshold event.

This is the charter at cosmic scale. Forcing a Zenith is deciding a world's meaning is worth ending it — the exact sin, written large. Aethris is what one finished glyph looks like. The completed spell is what all of them, linked, looks like.

For monoliths-as-archive (the structural mechanism Vecna is exploiting), see Physics → Monoliths. For Zenith-as-threshold, see Physics → Zeniths.

D — The End Fixed

Vecna completes the inscription. Every required glyph is written and claimed; the marks are linked into a single channel; the final threshold is forced. Reality itself crosses a Zenith — not a world's, but Existence's — and the finished spell routes all of reality through him. He becomes the answer to who gets to be real.

Failure state has a name: Eve of Ruin If the inscription completes, the world becomes the published apocalypse (Vecna's Ritual of Remaking). This is not a module we might run — it is the shape of the world if the crew does nothing, bearing down as the future. Every node they fail to contest moves the world one glyph closer to that book.

Useful corollary: the official Eve of Ruin is widely criticized as a disconnected teleport-rail. Take its stakes and set pieces; leave its structure. Our living-world architecture is the correction to exactly what people disliked about it. The Ascension World can be run as a standing glimpse of that failure state — a place where the inscription already completed, locally. See Back-burner for the full late-arc material.

The node ladder Fixed order

The only villain-planning that exists in advance. Order is fixed; timing is flexible. Crew enters between nodes 1 and 2. Arc II lives in nodes 2 and 3.

#NodeWhat it isStatus
1 Locate the glyphs Which monoliths exist, where, and which key one anchors the inscription. Done at Academy close
2 Claim the existing glyphs Reach and secure standing monoliths. Crew enters here
3 Inscribe the missing glyphs Force Zeniths where a glyph is needed but absent. Where worlds die; where the crew sees the cost. Arc II
4 Link the inscription Connect the glyphs into one channel. The convergence. Arc II → III hinge
5 Complete it Force the final threshold. Reality crosses. Vecna becomes the answer. D · Eve of Ruin

The crew can delay, witness, or complicate any node. They cannot make the network stop working toward the next one. You know the order; you never know the exact when. Arc II is a long runway — the channel doesn't even form until late.

The planning method Standing rule

The network is planned at three resolutions, and only three. This is the discipline that keeps the living world from collapsing into a flowchart.

The End (D) — fixed
Never changes. The gravity everything falls toward.
The Nodes — fixed in sequence, flexible in timing
The load-bearing steps above. The crew bends when, never whether.
The Next Immediate Step — the only thing planned in detail
Where the network is reaching right now, concrete enough to run. The moment it resolves — with or without the party — author the next immediate step in response to what actually happened.

Everything between the nodes — couriers, side-deals, small moves — is improvised at the table and written down only after it happens, as history. Never pre-planned, because the party and the world are what fill that space.

The shape, always A is the plan. B happened (the party acted). C is the network's response. They still have to reach D. Maintain three things only: the goal, the ordered node list, and one index card that says "what the network is doing this minute." After each voyage, throw away the card and write the next one from what the crew did.

The Network

What they are, in one line: the convergence of everyone trying to own the power to decide what's real, with Vecna as the mind who understands they're all doing the same thing and wants to be the single point it all passes through.

Fleet Intelligence does not know this is one thing. Neither does any institution. The crew assembles it before anyone — because they're the ones who feel what's at stake.

What they just did — the Academy operation Locked

The thefts and sabotage at Caelestis were never the point — and there were two thieves in the same chaos.

Vocath Locked

A mercane with a personal/commercial grudge — not a cultist. His real aim is to disgrace Mirt enough to prise him loose from the school's protection so someone else finishes him. The school is Mirt's shield — Mirt has many enemies; as headmaster he's institutionally protected. Vocath doesn't need to kill Mirt or destroy the school. He needs to wreck Mirt's standing.

The thefts and sabotage are manufactured noise — cover that also wrecks Mirt's standing. He happily owns the grudge because it's perfect cover. Every incident tests: does this make Mirt's school look like a failure and Mirt like a fool?

The network — the hidden hand Locked

The hidden hand using Vocath's operation. They wanted the Academy distracted while their own quiet theft ran underneath, via a bribed dock seam (the re-labeler; Blip not complicit; the Joffrey/Jeffrey thread). The dock seam re-labels restricted crates as routine freight. The disc-marked crate cache at the Sky Dock pools the cover-loot.

So there are two thieves in the same chaos: Vocath's people grab visible grudge-loot (pistol, supplies, disc-marked crates); the network's saboteur lifts the one thing that actually matters underneath.

The artifact (the network's true prize)

The network's true prize is an artifact no one at Caelestis understood. Miscatalogued, shelved as a curio, lifted quietly because you can't guard what you don't know is precious. This explains the "things are missing, nobody's sure what" texture: the academy notices odd inventory gaps (cover-thefts make that normal), but the one gap that matters is a thing nobody can describe.

What it does
Points the network toward the next glyph (node-1 output → node-2 input).
Why only Caelestis had it
It was here, unrecognized. The risky in-person op happened here, not via a black-market buy, because the artifact is non-fungible to this location.
Literacy-asymmetry
The network knows what the artifact is for; Caelestis and the crew do not. If the crew foils the heist and ends up holding the artifact (or half of it), they hold the answer and can't read it yet — the engine of the Arc II decode.

The visible navigational charts were Vocath's grudge-loot (cover, possible misdirection — the crew may chase these first). The artifact is the network's true prize, and it routes (eventually) toward Cairn Station.

What step Vecna is on — locating → claiming

The network has just finished node 1 (locate the glyphs) and is reaching for node 2 (claim/inscribe the next glyph). This is the seam the crew enters at. Nothing is consummated; the inscription is far from complete. That's exactly why a Level 4+ crew can meaningfully interfere — they're present while the ink is still being chosen, years before the spell could close.

One glyph is already "live" — one point on the stolen reconnaissance where the network has moved from mapping to operation (claiming a standing monolith, or preparing a Zenith to inscribe a missing one). This is what the crew can stumble into mid-chapter early in Arc II. Keep which one floating — commit it to wherever the crew's first heading points, so the network is always already a step ahead of them at their first real destination.

The Fork — the Academy finale set the network's tempo

Enforcing the no-railroad rule: the players had agency over the cosmic plot whether or not they understood it. The graduation raid resolves into one of two world-states. This is a perturbation, not a setback — either way the network has the artifact and is going to the next glyph. What the players changed is how fast and how quietly.

State A — Ahead Operation completed cleanly

Artifact left intact; quiet exit; no loose ends. The network reaches the live glyph undisturbed and confident. When the crew first encounters network work, they're walking into something already underway — they're behind, and they feel it.

Tone
The door's already been kicked in by someone else. Who was here before us?
Arc II is a CHASE
They can't undo the head start; they're now the only ones tracking it.
State B — Delayed Players perturbed the extraction

The crew fouled the clean exit — kept Miken alive and talking, left a witness the network can't account for, slowed the hand-off, or ended up holding the artifact (or half of it) themselves. The network moves slower and warier — checking whether it was compromised, moving more carefully. It still gets there (or re-acquires what it needs). This buys the crew time and paints a target: the network now has reason to care about this specific crew.

Tone
The crew arrives early enough to catch the network arriving. Contemporaneous, not behind. We got here in time — this once.
Arc II is a CONTEST
If the crew holds the artifact, they hold the answer and can't read it yet — the literacy-asymmetry made literal, the engine of the Arc II decode. A flipped Miken (if alive) is a thread straight into the network's protocol.

Either way the spine holds. The plan is load-bearing; the players bend its tempo. State A = chase, State B = contest. Both are good. The players chose which. Confirm A or B after graduation runs; lock the tempo.

The Weather of Reality

Standing conditions of the Continuum under strain — background radiation the crew keeps half-noticing. The charter's question at cosmic scale, none requiring the network.

The Existence event still reverberates

The Axiom produces anomalies — generative (Gregory, the Latchling) and unmaking (clockwork horrors) — increasing in frequency, faintly, like aftershocks. The crew meets more anomalies than the network can account for, because the Axiom itself is restless. The pressure is real and ownerless; Vecna exploits a true condition, doesn't manufacture a fake one.

Astra's reach is widening (Casey's thread)

The wounded paracausal celestial whose voice reaches Casey through Polly is extending — each tether clarifies, each attuned constellation point lets Astra reach further. A second, independent cosmic pull with nothing to do with the network. Sometimes the strange thing at a destination is Astra, not Vecna.

Zeniths build on their own clocks

Worlds rise toward thresholds naturally — intensification is an axiom. Some pressure is just a world doing what worlds do, no villain. The Watchers monitor several. The network wants some. Telling which is which is the crew's actual work — and the thing only they can do.

The "this already happened" anchor worlds

Two reachable worlds that show the crew their own future:

Aethris (Sol's world)
A completed manufactured Zenith — one finished glyph. Someone forced this world past its threshold and it "succeeded"; Sol is the one survivor, the loose end. The Prism Ascendant still stands. Someone else deciding. Full entry at World → Aethris.
The Ascension World Specifics pending
A reachable world where Vecna once forced a threshold and won, locally — a small, personal ascension (not the canonical Sigil event) that left the world a scar: where "who decides what's real" was decided permanently in one being's favor and everything else became a footnote to him. Standing on it is standing inside the failure state — a local Eve of Ruin you can walk. Canon permits this; Vecna's ascension origins are deliberately diffuse. Hold specifics for Arc II-late / III, but make it namable on the charts now as an unmarked point that resonates wrong. Vecna deciding.

Aethris is someone else deciding. The Ascension World is Vecna deciding. Together they are the two faces of the warning — and why the crew fights.

Arc Architecture

The published Lore Bible's four-arc structure, translated into concrete near-term trajectory for this table. This is a projection, not a plan. It bends to the road taken.

Arc I — Spelljammer Academy (now → ~Level 4) Nearly complete

The published modules are the tutorial. Their real function in hindsight: every "ordinary" incident (the thefts, the neogi, the sabotage, the missing ships, the clockwork horrors, the H'Catha meteor) is a component — a small expression of the same architecture, seeded so the crew can later assemble the pattern themselves.

  • Voyage 004: Fire in the Galley cold open — Ryeback dies, Miken's betrayal lands. Then Behold H'Catha, the final published module: recover the adamantine meteor.

End of Arc I — The Bridge Out (the Graduation)

The crew graduates as Driftwardens (the in-world name for the Spelljammer Corps — the selective operational arm). The hinge is that they are kept together as a unit rather than scattered into the Fleet — because they are the pattern. Mirt's own line at the Convening becomes the justification: trouble finds them.

The meteor beat: handed over, then reclassified above their clearance in the graduation debrief. They don't get it back; they just register it mattered. One sentence. The breadcrumb that retroactively loads the entire tutorial.

Arc II — Open Wildspace / Driftwarden Operations (~Level 5–10) The sandbox

This is the sandbox. The crew takes missions: transport, escort, recovery, investigation, assisting Watchers. The structure of each mission is local problem first, deeper pattern second. They meet the cult, the smuggler, the corrupt official — and only later does the shape underneath become legible.

This is where the seeds (below) get pulled. The players choose which threads to follow; those become the spine of Arc II. Watchers begin appearing more often. The Restorers stay entirely offstage.

Arc II's operational structure — Caelestis, the Fleet, the Watchers, the handler, the mission types, hooks — is the next section.

Arc III — Penultimate Countermove (~Level 11–15)

The pattern becomes undeniable. Allied factions begin acting with intent. The Restorers become legible as a competing hidden agenda (not allies). Focus shifts to ritual nodes, compromised archives, redirecting Zenith outcomes, and the painful triage of which crises can be stopped.

Arc IV — Endgame Against Vecna (Level 15+)

Dismantling the ritual architecture. The two layers finally merge: the crew that started as cadets now acts at cosmological scale. Travel between monolith sites, defense of the Core, confrontation with a half-completed overwrite of Existence.

Arc II Structure

The operational layer — how the post-Academy game runs.

Caelestis — one station, two functions

Caelestis is a single station containing both the Academy wing (Mirt's domain — training, cadets, graduation) and the Fleet operations wing (the handler's domain — the contract board, the docks, the working crews). Graduation = crossing the station, not relocating. The cadets cross from the school side to the operations side; the threshold is internal. Keeps the name and the existing art. The operations side is part base, part market — where the space-bar-adjacent social layer lives.

Full Caelestis location entry: World → Caelestis.

The Fleet — what it is

Self-made and non-governmental
Someone built this organization; no council of worlds chartered it. Authority is self-bestowed by reputation, not granted. There is no UN-of-planets behind it.
For-hire
Contracts are load-bearing, not a side hustle. The crew's missions mix internal Fleet assignments and external contracts the Fleet brokered — from the crew's seat, both feel like "go here, do this, get paid in standing and slack."
Covert
Most worlds don't know Wildspace exists. The Fleet doesn't descend over cities revealing space travel; it blends in, deals with the few locals who are read in, leaves without rewriting a civilization's reality. Built-in recurring constraint: fix the problem without revealing the sky.
Respected pirate-fleet command, not a navy
Organized but not military. Crews are trusted operators, not soldiers. Rank buys freedom and range, not just authority.
The leash is geographic and loosens with rank
Early crews are bounded to a system (or defined area). A mission says "do this, here, by this window" — inside that box, the ship and the time are theirs. Deliver on schedule and the slack between is pure sandbox. Rank up → the box expands → longer hauls, further systems, more rope. Freedom is the progression reward.

The Watchers — in Arc II

A known, respected partner organization. More structured and more morally forward than the Fleet. Authority is divine-by-belief (the All-Father). The Fleet routinely works with them and transports them. Productive friction in play: the Fleet cares about the delivered outcome; the Watchers care about the right one. Not antagonism — values in tension.

They need each other: the Watchers have the mandate and the moral clarity but rely on the Fleet for reach (ships, cover, logistics). The Fleet has the muscle and the deniability but no divine mission. The crew lives at that seam. Full entry at World → Watchers.

The handler — a new character + a job board

Mirt runs the Academy, not Arc II orders. A new Fleet figure runs this crew (the Variks slot). The board is the menu (curates choice, kills analysis paralysis); the handler is the maître d' who pre-pulls jobs they think the crew will like, vouches for them, covers for them. Both, not either. The board-poster and the crew-runner need not be the same person.

Missions — the nudge, not the content

  • The mission is a nudge to a destination. Its only job is to give a reason to point the crew somewhere when they stall. It has a timeframe (sometimes loose, sometimes consequence-bearing, never punishing). The story is what's already waiting at the destination.
  • They arrive mid-chapter. The world was deciding who counts before they got there and will keep deciding after they leave. Their interaction continues a story already in motion — never page one. If they bail, it resolves or rolls on without them.
  • Pressure and direction, never "tonight's content." Threads pull; the crew chooses which to step into. Inside a chosen situation they're committed until it concludes (scenes have walls — that's not railroading). Stepping in and out is the freedom.
  • ~One in three sheds residue. Honest jobs aren't filler — they make the residue legible as residue. If everything connects, nothing does.
  • Movable story. Build a situation's shape (who's deciding, who's decided-about, what it costs); let the world supply the costume. If the crew detours, move the door and re-skin it. Only world-specific story (Aethris, Ascension World, Tumak's homeworld, Casey's Viren) stays nailed down.

Scope & geography

Realmspace + one neighboring system. Two systems max early — the sandbox stays holdable. Within-sphere travel is fast (days between worlds, an asteroid/waystation en route — the cowboy-downtime geography). Cross-sphere (phlogiston) travel is the long-haul stuff deferred until the campaign earns it; it maps onto the rank-leash (senior crews do long hauls). The void between planets is itself content — derelicts, waystations, other ships are the Spelljammer "road encounter."

Note: the published Spelljammer setting is deliberately under-structured about campaign play. The books give ships/species/setting and almost no sandbox guidance; the one published adventure is a hard rail. This framework fills a vacuum the books left.

Hooks & Threads

A hook is a live situation at a destination that the crew can step into. The metaphor: a mission is the nudge that points the crew at a place; the hook is what's already happening there that they can walk into.

Hooks come from your Mighty-Nein architecture — they arrive mid-chapter, the story is already in motion, their interaction continues it. Open the hook → engage the situation. Walk past → the situation continues without them.

The session-shape: mission (the nudge) → crew arrives at destination → hook(s) live there → crew picks which to engage. Each hook is its own situation. Some route back to the network; some don't.

The five hook types

1. Network, directly

The live glyph; a chart-route waystation; cargo that's secretly their logistics. Routes to the clock.

Rare — overuse and the pattern stops being a discovery.

2. Network, indirectly — an ally or tool

A Syndicate cell moving relics that happen to be archived souls; an Archivist meddling at a monolith site for their own reasons that serve the network by accident.

Connection without coordination — the "ally of the network" texture.

3. A different cosmic pull

Astra reaching through a world; a natural Zenith building; a fresh Existence-anomaly. Cosmic stakes, not Vecna.

Keeps "not everything is the network" true and load-bearing.

4. Purely local — its own microcosm

A corrupt official, a forced meaning, a being treated as cargo, at human scale with no cosmic tie.

The honest jobs and the small-scale version of the question. Roughly two of every three hooks. They make the others legible.

5. Personal — a PC's thread reaches out

Tumak's homeworld memory triggered; Casey hearing Polly clearer; a CRC sighting for Sol; the Latchling for Gregory.

The pull, made local.

The portable-hook library The actual portable hook-shapes — situation templates you costume to any world — are the next doc to build. The taxonomy above locks the kinds; the library builds the inventory.

The Seed Inventory

Every planted hook, what it could become if pulled, and how it routes back to the spine. Update after each voyage: move seeds from Planted (in the world, not yet pursued) to Pulled (players engaging) to Resolved.

The give-and-take rule A seed's "if pulled" is a direction, not a script. If players pull it, that direction becomes real and gets developed. If they pull it somewhere you didn't anticipate, bend the destination — but keep the route back to the spine. No seed is wasted; unpulled seeds stay planted for later.

The Convergence Rule

The procedure that lets players go anywhere without breaking the spine. Run any new location, crisis, faction, or artifact through these — if you can answer them, the campaign stays aligned even when the road surprises you.

  1. Is this event building toward, reacting to, or recovering from a threshold (Zenith)?
  2. Which faction notices it first, and why?
  3. Does it intensify, converge, escalate, or fracture existing pressures?
  4. Would Vecna benefit if everyone misread this as a local problem?
  5. What would a Watcher do here?
  6. If a hidden Restorer interest exists here, what would it want?
  7. What part of reality is actually at stake beneath the surface story?

Two standing commitments to the table

Never hard-block a direction for lack of prep

If the players pull a thread you didn't plan, the thread becomes real and bends toward the spine. The overarching story is told along the road they choose.

Every choice returns something

Not always obvious at first — but every pursued seed yields information, and that information always, eventually, points inward toward reality is being stressed.

DM Craft Principles

Carry-forward principles for running this campaign. Not philosophical — these are the operational rules that keep the table at its best.

Pressure comes from the task, not the monsters

A good encounter's tension is what they must accomplish under fire, not enemies existing to be hit. The helm-install-in-the-cone is the model.

Use the full creature kit

Stop crutching on one attack (esp. humanoids). Synergize multiple creatures' abilities. The wisps (shelled-screech + hollowed-converge) are the practice case.

Layer trade-offs, never a free win

Every solution opens a new cost (kill the cone → traps arm). "Oh, that makes sense" not "gotcha."

Reward learning the space

Traps/mechanics seen on earlier decks → recognized later → award the disarm/counter. Players taking notes and remembering layout should be rewarded.

Hide the map

Grid for combat only; theatre-of-mind for exploration so the dark/shafts/gravity-flip do their work.

Push class-as-identity off the battlefield

Engineer non-combat problems a Paladin/Sorcerer/Druid solves by being one. The anti-magic room is the ultimate version (casters must find non-magical answers).

Pace in segments

Clear/cross a deck, bunker, rest, plan. Don't play every fight; vary tempo. Sandbox-in-a-box.

No PC death when a player is absent

Standing rule.

The Crossroads Principle

The realized why of running in Wildspace: it's the multiverse's junction. So you can run an original homebrew campaign studded with breadcrumbs to the published D&D world — things that mean nothing to the characters but everything to the players. Knowing D&D is a second reward layer.
