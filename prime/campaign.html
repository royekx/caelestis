Who decides what's real?

The North

Who decides what's real? That is the campaign, in five words.

The monoliths, the Zeniths, the network, the heist — all of it is the pressure that forces the question into the open. The question is the thing. "Real" means: counts; persists by right; gets to exist as itself rather than as someone else's function, error, property, or permission slip. The whole campaign is a long argument about who gets to grant that, withhold it, or claim it — and whether anyone has the right to decide it for anyone else.

Why this crew

They are not six adventurers who met in a tavern. They are six people each handed a verdict — told, in some specific way, that they don't fully count — slowly deciding, together, to send those verdicts back.

Gregory — inventory
A made thing asking whether a made thing can be a person.
Sol — error
A man his world declared a mistake, existing through sheer refusal to stop.
Boogie — container
Becoming a vessel for the unmade; does it matter that something survived in him?
Tumak — constructed self
Two people as one, asking whether a self you built is still real or a lie that works.
Casey — instrument
Guided toward a purpose he didn't choose, by a voice he trusts but doesn't understand.
Bartholomew — the thing he won't look at
Circling a shape already inside him, deciding whether to recognize what he already is.

Same question, six keys. That unity is the engine to lean on, not a coincidence to hide.

Why Vecna

Vecna is the negative space of the crew — the answer each of them fears is true, scaled to cosmic and made permanent:

You are real only if something with power says so.

He wants to fracture the Axiom of Convergence so the Continuum re-equilibrates around him. If it works, all of Existence is routed through him as a condition of being. He needs no manifesto; he is the verdict each PC already heard once, in their own voice, on their worst day. The crew doesn't fight him because he's evil. They fight him because they know, personally, exactly what he's selling.

The found family is the mechanism, not the decoration

You become real by being seen as real by people who choose you.

The crew is each other's proof. That's what a found family is here — the literal mechanism of the theme, not a side-warmth. The emotional spine and the thematic spine are the same spine. Pay off the bond and you've paid off the campaign.

Not everything is Vecna — and that's the point

The recurring misread to avoid: "Vecna is behind everything." He isn't. The truth is worse and more useful:

Everyone is doing a small version of what Vecna does big. The corrupt official deciding a person doesn't count. The cult deciding their meaning is worth a world. A Watcher deciding a civilization can't be saved. The Syndicate treating beings as cargo. None of these are Vecna — they're the ordinary face of the same impulse, which is exactly why Vecna can hide inside it. The crew learns to see the cosmic by first learning to see the small.

The mid-tier factions are a spectrum of the same sin — each a different answer to who gets to decide what's real:

FactionTheir answer
ConcordanceWe do — we enforce which reality is legitimate.
ArchivistsWe do — we hoard what's allowed to be remembered.
SyndicateNobody, and that's profitable — beings are cargo.
Fracture CultWe do — we force meaning by breaking the world into a sharper shape.
Rogue WatcherI do — I decide who survives, for their own good.
The crewThe thing itself does. You don't get to decide that for someone.

They don't coordinate. They converge — all reaching for the same power from different directions. The convergence is the plot; the question is why it matters.

The table test

For any situation, NPC, world, mission, or idea, ask one thing:

The test Who here is trying to decide what's real — for themselves or for someone else — and what does it cost?

Answerable → it's a Caelestis story; build it. Not answerable → it's texture, not story; run it light. This is what replaces the module. You never invent "what happens" — you ask who's deciding and who's being decided about, and the scene appears.

The three zoom levels

The whole architecture is one question at three scales:

Cosmic
The network / Vecna's inscription. The question at the scale of all reality.
Local
A world, faction, or mission. The question at the scale of a place and its people.
Personal
Each PC's arc. The question at the scale of one self.

A great session stacks all three in one scene: a PC's private verdict surfaces (personal) inside a local crisis (local) that turns out to be a fingerprint of the cosmic war (cosmic). When those three rhyme in one moment, the campaign is working.

The Weather of Reality

Standing conditions of the Continuum under strain — background radiation the crew keeps half-noticing. The charter's question at cosmic scale, none requiring the network.

Continuity is under live stress

The Continuum is producing anomalies — generative (Gregory, the Latchling) and unmaking (clockwork horrors) — increasing in frequency, faintly, like a system under strain. This is not background residue from a past event. It is live consequence of present pressure: as more monoliths come online — claimed, tainted, or newly responded into being — Continuity strains a little harder to maintain coherence, and the strain spits out anomalies. The pressure is real, growing, and Vecna is the cause — though no faction has yet linked the symptoms to him.

Player-facing tells: the increase in anomalies the crew meets; unmaking events (things erased, forgotten, absences); awakenings like the Latchling; small reality-coherence slips (a place that "shouldn't" exist now does; a person remembered by everyone who turns out to have never been there). The Watchers notice rising Continuity-pressure across many worlds at once and don't yet know why.

Astra's reach is widening (Casey's thread)

The wounded paracausal celestial whose voice reaches Casey through Polly is extending — each tether clarifies, each attuned constellation point lets Astra reach further. A second, independent cosmic pull with nothing to do with the network. Sometimes the strange thing at a destination is Astra, not Vecna.

Zeniths build on their own clocks

Worlds rise toward thresholds naturally and constantly — Convergence drives them. Civilizations peak. Wars escalate. Rituals saturate. Most Zeniths the Watchers attend to have nothing to do with the network and never will. The Continuum produces them anyway. The Order has been managing this forever; Vecna is a recent complication on top of a much older system.

Telling which Zeniths are his and which are the universe doing its ordinary business is the crew's actual work — and the thing only they, embedded with the Fleet, traveling everywhere, are positioned to do.

The "this already happened" anchor worlds

Two reachable worlds that show the crew their own future:

Aethris (Sol's world)
A world that reached its own Zenith — under pressure that the network encouraged at the critical moment. The Prism Ascendant is the architecture of the Zenith Aethris built for itself (a Tymora-worship civilization compressing destiny into ritual). What Vecna did was time the threshold so the responding monolith would land exactly where his figure needed a mark. Aethris's society did the work of approaching the threshold; the network only nudged. Sol is the one survivor, the loose end. The Prism Ascendant still stands. Aethris is what one of his points looks like after the world that produced it died. Full entry at Realms → Aethris.
The Ascension World Specifics pending
A reachable world where Vecna once forced a threshold and won, locally — a small, personal ascension (not the canonical Sigil event) that left the world a scar: where "who decides what's real" was decided permanently in one being's favor and everything else became a footnote to him. Standing on it is standing inside the failure state — a local Eve of Ruin you can walk. Canon permits this; Vecna's ascension origins are deliberately diffuse. Hold specifics for Arc II-late / III, but make it namable on the charts now as an unmarked point that resonates wrong. Vecna deciding.

Aethris is someone else deciding. The Ascension World is Vecna deciding. Together they are the two faces of the warning — and why the crew fights.

The Act Map

The campaign at a glance. Each Act is a focusing of "who decides what's real?" at a different scale. Beats shift as the road is taken; the questions don't.

Arc I — Spelljammer Academy Live · Module 3 of published Level 1–4

The question: Who counts as a real crew? Cadets are inventory until they prove they matter. The found family forms inside structures that don't yet see them. The cosmology is rumor; the institution is real; the work is concrete.

Tone: military structure, cadet camaraderie, practical training, the exciting danger of Wildspace. Sabotage breathing in the background, not yet legible. Living sandbox inside and around the Academy — Caelestis is more than a campus, and the crew already drifts beyond it when the missions allow.

Done

  • The crew assembles at Caelestis. Trial by Fire establishes them as cadets and as a unit.
  • First contact with the strange. Boogie's nucleus speaks. Casey's wardrobe vision points past Hakatha toward Viren. Sol survives the failed ritual on Aethris before the campaign opens; arrives already a loose end.
  • The thefts begin. Sabotage plates, the stolen pistol, Saerthe's missing ship. Two thieves in the same chaos — Vocath's grudge-op and the network underneath it — but neither yet exposed.
  • The neogi incident. The transmutation sigil under the neogi's chitin matches the sabotage plates. The pattern-engine seed is planted; nobody has connected it yet.
  • Voyage 003 in progress. Tyrant ship op stretched to 2–3 sessions for breathing room. Wizpop surfaces Gregory's paracausality through a peer ("You cast that — we don't — what are you?"). The clockwork horrors and resonance-cores enter the seed inventory. Beholder absence is logged as a question, not an answer.

To hit

  • The Convening — the inflection beat. Mirt and the Bridge reframe the crew as a pattern, not witnesses. The sabotage mystery surfaces enough that the crew starts seeing the connections themselves.
  • Sandbox interlude. Time off-script. The crew moves through Caelestis as a living place — Sea Dock, Sky Dock, Weeping Goddess, Stores. Gregory's Latchling arc activates. Rindle Gearloft surfaces as the social obstacle. Miken's hushed contact gets noticed or doesn't — both lead somewhere.
  • Realmspace Sortie. First real mission off-station. Tone shift: the Academy is no longer the world. The Foundling is the crew's ship for it.
  • Fire in the Galley. Ryeback's death. Miken's betrayal lands hard. The pattern becomes undeniable to the crew.
  • Behold H'Catha — the final published module. Recover the adamantine meteor. Tyrant ship disguise to reach H'Catha. Sol's first CRC encounter lands in this window as an objective-based escape, not a fight: "Sol Fortuna. You are required."
  • The Bridge Out — Graduation. Crew becomes Driftwardens. They're kept together as a unit rather than scattered. The meteor is handed over and reclassified above their clearance in the debrief — the breadcrumb that retroactively loads the whole Act. Mirt's reputation outcome resolves (open thread). The graduation Fork sets Arc II's tempo (State A chase / State B contest).

PC arcs in this Act: Gregory recognizes himself as anomalous (Wizpop surface; Latchling first contact). Sol's Oath of Misfortune lives in his hands; the CRC introduces itself. Boogie's nucleus accretes (half-ship, logbook; the corrupted shard's Pulse). Casey's wardrobe vision and the Sky Dock presence plant Polly. Tumak's runes surface near cosmic threats. Bartholomew is observed — creature design for the Almosts stays open.

Faction moves in this Act: the Watchers stay near-invisible; Ezra signposted as a future tie-in. The network operates in cover; the crew sees Vocath's grudge but not the hand beneath it.

Arc II — Open Wildspace Upcoming Level 5–10

The question: Who decides what's worth saving? Every mission is a triage. Each faction has its own answer. The crew develops theirs by acting — not by deciding philosophically, but by choosing in the moment.

Tone: the sandbox breathes. Missions as nudges, hooks live at destinations, five hook types in rotation. The Fleet's leash is geographic and loosens with rank. Watchers begin appearing more often. The Restorers stay entirely offstage. Most of what the crew encounters is not the network — and that's load-bearing.

The seam at Act open

  • One point is already live on the network's figure (committed to wherever the crew first heads). They walk into mid-chapter — they're behind or contemporaneous depending on the Fork.
  • Continuity is under live stress (see the Weather). Anomalies are increasing. The Watchers notice rising Continuity-pressure across many worlds at once and don't yet know why.
  • The crew holds (or doesn't hold) the artifact. If they have it, the literacy-asymmetry is the engine of the Act: they hold the answer and can't read it yet.

Broad strokes

  • Mission rhythm establishes. A handler curates a board. Roughly one in three jobs sheds residue connecting to the spine. Honest jobs aren't filler — they make the residue legible as residue.
  • The crew converges on the pattern faster than any institution. Because they're embedded with the Fleet, traveling everywhere, present at incidents that don't share filing cabinets. The Watchers see Continuity-pressure rising; the crew sees the shape of why.
  • Aethris becomes reachable. Sol's homeworld surfaces as a destination. The Fortuned pursue him; the CRC recurs as foil, not boss. Am I a person or a function?
  • Casey's Viren pull builds. Polly clarifies one notch. Starchildren as a category begins to come into focus. Astra's reach is widening — a second cosmic pull, unrelated to the network.
  • Gregory's Calibration finds more anomalies. The Latchling resolves; new ones surface. He becomes the crew's instrument for sensing the hidden.
  • The Latchling arc resolves within Act II (the journey is the arc, not the destination).
  • Cairn Station enters the chart. The artifact's trail eventually points there. Astral dreadnought territory.
  • A natural Zenith the crew tries to save. Cosmic stakes, not Vecna. To prove the question matters at this scale even when the antagonist isn't present.
  • A network-engineered Zenith the crew arrives at too late. They see what a manufactured one looks like up close. Aethris-pattern, fresh. Where worlds die because the figure needs a dot there.

PC arcs in this Act: Sol confronts Aethris; the CRC recurs. Gregory's role as crew-instrument solidifies via the Latchling and successor anomalies. Boogie's archival residue starts mattering to him personally. Casey's stellar chart fills in; Polly named or not depending on table. Tumak's homeworld memory triggers. Bartholomew's portrayal is observed; the Almosts creature design moves toward locked.

Faction moves in this Act: the Watchers become a working partner the crew interacts with regularly. The Concordance, Fracture Cult, Archivists, and Syndicate appear on missions as the spectrum-of-the-same-sin — local first, pattern second. The Restorers stay invisible. The network's hand becomes provable; Fleet Intelligence still doesn't see one enemy.

Arc III — The Pattern Shape only Level 11–15

The question: Who's been deciding this all along? The discovery arc. The hidden hand becomes visible; institutions catch up to what the crew already knew; the crew catches up to what they already half-suspected.

Shape: the pattern becomes undeniable. The crew assembles the truth about the network before any institution does, because that's the structural fact of where they sit. Allied factions begin acting with intent rather than reacting. The Restorers become legible as a competing hidden agenda — not allies. Focus shifts to ritual nodes, compromised archives, redirecting Zenith outcomes, and the painful triage of which crises can be stopped.

Anchor shapes (not beats, yet)

  • The figure becomes legible. The crew can read it. They see what shape Vecna has been drawing and what it would do if completed.
  • Vecna's own origin surfaces. His ascension as a Zenith. The monolith that responded. The discovery he made by looking back from godhood. (DM understanding becomes player-knowable here, late.) The crew understands that he is one being inside the system who learned to read it.
  • The Restorers' agenda comes into focus. Not as enemies, not as allies — as a third force with its own answer to the campaign question, also reaching for cosmic-scale intervention.
  • Aethris closes. Sol's arc resolves to a chosen identity rather than an inherited one.
  • The Ascension World becomes reachable. The crew walks the failure state. Local Eve of Ruin. Vecna deciding made into ground.
  • The dual attack is named. The crew understands the spell and the stress are the same work. Convergence is the Axiom under strain. Continuity is what's at stake.

PC arcs in this Act: Boogie's shard becomes the literal proof-of-concept for removing a point from the figure — the cross-campaign answer surfaces. Casey's Starchildren framing fully named; the cost of tethering surfaces (Fjord's choice). Tumak's homecoming approaches and is recognized as a Zenith risk. Bartholomew's Almosts become known and used.

Faction moves in this Act: the Order acts at strategic scale, no longer just monitoring. The Watchers operate openly with the crew. Restorers come into focus as competitors for the same intervention space. The Bridge fractures internally over how to respond.

Arc IV — The Endgame Shape only Level 15+

The question: Who gets to decide for everyone, forever? The final form. Vecna's bid is the literal worst-case answer to the campaign question, made cosmic. The crew's answer is the opposite: no one. The thing itself decides. You don't get to do that to anyone.

Shape: dismantling the figure. The two campaign layers — the practical Spelljammer crew that began as cadets, and the cosmic stakes that have grown around them — finally merge. They act at cosmological scale because no one else is positioned to. Travel between monolith sites, defense of the Core, confrontation with a half-completed spell-and-fracture working.

Anchor shapes

  • The race against the Fracture. Convergence is approaching the point where the Shock cascades. Every action the crew takes either delays it or accelerates it; every monolith they reach matters.
  • The figure is contested point by point. Some monoliths are reached and unclaimed (returned to neutral function). Some are destroyed at terrible cost (Fonains-pattern). Some are denied to Vecna by being completed early (the crew finishes a Zenith on their terms, not his).
  • The final confrontation. Not "kill the lich." Stop the spell from completing while the Fracture is held off. Vecna's defeat is structural — interrupting the working, not destroying the caster.
  • The Doomed Forgotten Realms remains the failure state. If the crew fails, the world becomes that one — the question answered yes, one being decides, and it is him.
  • The crew's own answer becomes the ending. Whatever they choose to be — to each other, to the worlds they passed through, to the question — is what the campaign closes on. Not a fight resolution. A verdict resolution.

Open question (don't decide now): is the campaign's true ending "prevent Eve of Ruin" or "survive Eve of Ruin"? Read the table near node 4 of the inscription. The crew's posture by Arc IV opening determines which is honest.

Arc II Structure

The operational layer — how the post-Academy game runs.

Caelestis — one station, two functions

Caelestis is a single station containing both the Academy wing (Mirt's domain — training, cadets, graduation) and the Fleet operations wing (the handler's domain — the contract board, the docks, the working crews). Graduation = crossing the station, not relocating. The cadets cross from the school side to the operations side; the threshold is internal. Keeps the name and the existing art. The operations side is part base, part market — where the space-bar-adjacent social layer lives.

Full Caelestis location entry: Realms → Caelestis.

The Fleet — what it is

Self-made and non-governmental
Someone built this organization; no council of worlds chartered it. Authority is self-bestowed by reputation, not granted. There is no UN-of-planets behind it.
For-hire
Contracts are load-bearing, not a side hustle. The crew's missions mix internal Fleet assignments and external contracts the Fleet brokered — from the crew's seat, both feel like "go here, do this, get paid in standing and slack."
Covert
Most worlds don't know Wildspace exists. The Fleet doesn't descend over cities revealing space travel; it blends in, deals with the few locals who are read in, leaves without rewriting a civilization's reality. Built-in recurring constraint: fix the problem without revealing the sky.
Respected pirate-fleet command, not a navy
Organized but not military. Crews are trusted operators, not soldiers. Rank buys freedom and range, not just authority.
The leash is geographic and loosens with rank
Early crews are bounded to a system (or defined area). A mission says "do this, here, by this window" — inside that box, the ship and the time are theirs. Deliver on schedule and the slack between is pure sandbox. Rank up → the box expands → longer hauls, further systems, more rope. Freedom is the progression reward.

The Watchers — in Arc II

A known, respected partner organization. More structured and more morally forward than the Fleet. Authority is divine-by-belief (the All-Father). The Fleet routinely works with them and transports them. Productive friction in play: the Fleet cares about the delivered outcome; the Watchers care about the right one. Not antagonism — values in tension.

They need each other: the Watchers have the mandate and the moral clarity but rely on the Fleet for reach (ships, cover, logistics). The Fleet has the muscle and the deniability but no divine mission. The crew lives at that seam. Full entry at Factions → Watchers.

The handler — a new character + a job board

Mirt runs the Academy, not Arc II orders. A new Fleet figure runs this crew (the Variks slot). The board is the menu (curates choice, kills analysis paralysis); the handler is the maître d' who pre-pulls jobs they think the crew will like, vouches for them, covers for them. Both, not either. The board-poster and the crew-runner need not be the same person.

Missions — the nudge, not the content

  • The mission is a nudge to a destination. Its only job is to give a reason to point the crew somewhere when they stall. It has a timeframe (sometimes loose, sometimes consequence-bearing, never punishing). The story is what's already waiting at the destination.
  • They arrive mid-chapter. The world was deciding who counts before they got there and will keep deciding after they leave. Their interaction continues a story already in motion — never page one. If they bail, it resolves or rolls on without them.
  • Pressure and direction, never "tonight's content." Threads pull; the crew chooses which to step into. Inside a chosen situation they're committed until it concludes (scenes have walls — that's not railroading). Stepping in and out is the freedom.
  • ~One in three sheds residue. Honest jobs aren't filler — they make the residue legible as residue. If everything connects, nothing does.
  • Movable story. Build a situation's shape (who's deciding, who's decided-about, what it costs); let the world supply the costume. If the crew detours, move the door and re-skin it. Only world-specific story (Aethris, Ascension World, Tumak's homeworld, Casey's Viren) stays nailed down.

Scope & geography

Realmspace + one neighboring system. Two systems max early — the sandbox stays holdable. Within-sphere travel is fast (days between worlds, an asteroid/waystation en route — the cowboy-downtime geography). Cross-sphere (phlogiston) travel is the long-haul stuff deferred until the campaign earns it; it maps onto the rank-leash (senior crews do long hauls). The void between planets is itself content — derelicts, waystations, other ships are the Spelljammer "road encounter."

Note: the published Spelljammer setting is deliberately under-structured about campaign play. The books give ships/species/setting and almost no sandbox guidance; the one published adventure is a hard rail. This framework fills a vacuum the books left.

Hooks & Threads

A hook is a live situation at a destination that the crew can step into. The metaphor: a mission is the nudge that points the crew at a place; the hook is what's already happening there that they can walk into.

Hooks come from your Mighty-Nein architecture — they arrive mid-chapter, the story is already in motion, their interaction continues it. Open the hook → engage the situation. Walk past → the situation continues without them.

The session-shape: mission (the nudge) → crew arrives at destination → hook(s) live there → crew picks which to engage. Each hook is its own situation. Some route back to the network; some don't.

The five hook types

1. Network, directly

The live point on the figure; a chart-route waystation; cargo that's secretly their logistics. Routes to the clock.

Rare — overuse and the pattern stops being a discovery.

2. Network, indirectly — an ally or tool

A Syndicate cell moving relics that happen to be archived souls; an Archivist meddling at a monolith site for their own reasons that serve the network by accident.

Connection without coordination — the "ally of the network" texture.

3. A different cosmic pull

Astra reaching through a world; a natural Zenith building; a fresh Existence-anomaly. Cosmic stakes, not Vecna.

Keeps "not everything is the network" true and load-bearing.

4. Purely local — its own microcosm

A corrupt official, a forced meaning, a being treated as cargo, at human scale with no cosmic tie.

The honest jobs and the small-scale version of the question. Roughly two of every three hooks. They make the others legible.

5. Personal — a PC's thread reaches out

Tumak's homeworld memory triggered; Casey hearing Polly clearer; a CRC sighting for Sol; the Latchling for Gregory.

The pull, made local.

The Seed Inventory

The planted-hooks prep queue — every seed, what it becomes if pulled, and how it routes to the spine. Update after each session: move seeds from Planted (in the world, not yet pursued) to Pulled (players engaging) to Resolved. Live status also lives in the Campaign Tracker.

The give-and-take rule A seed's "if pulled" is a direction, not a script. If players pull it, that direction becomes real and gets developed. If they pull it somewhere you didn't anticipate, bend the destination — but keep the route back to the spine. No seed is wasted; unpulled seeds stay planted for later.

The Convergence Rule

The procedure that lets players go anywhere without breaking the spine. Run any new location, crisis, faction, or artifact through these — if you can answer them, the campaign stays aligned even when the road surprises you.

  1. Is this event building toward, reacting to, or recovering from a threshold (Zenith)?
  2. Which faction notices it first, and why?
  3. Does it intensify, converge, escalate, or fracture existing pressures?
  4. Would Vecna benefit if everyone misread this as a local problem?
  5. What would a Watcher do here?
  6. If a hidden Restorer interest exists here, what would it want?
  7. What part of reality is actually at stake beneath the surface story?

Two standing commitments to the table

Never hard-block a direction for lack of prep

If the players pull a thread you didn't plan, the thread becomes real and bends toward the spine. The overarching story is told along the road they choose.

Every choice returns something

Not always obvious at first — but every pursued seed yields information, and that information always, eventually, points inward toward reality is being stressed.

DM Craft Principles

Carry-forward principles for running this campaign. Not philosophical — these are the operational rules that keep the table at its best.

Pressure comes from the task, not the monsters

A good encounter's tension is what they must accomplish under fire, not enemies existing to be hit. The helm-install-in-the-cone is the model.

Use the full creature kit

Stop crutching on one attack (esp. humanoids). Synergize multiple creatures' abilities. The wisps (shelled-screech + hollowed-converge) are the practice case.

Layer trade-offs, never a free win

Every solution opens a new cost (kill the cone → traps arm). "Oh, that makes sense" not "gotcha."

Reward learning the space

Traps/mechanics seen on earlier decks → recognized later → award the disarm/counter. Players taking notes and remembering layout should be rewarded.

Hide the map

Grid for combat only; theatre-of-mind for exploration so the dark/shafts/gravity-flip do their work.

Push class-as-identity off the battlefield

Engineer non-combat problems a Paladin/Sorcerer/Druid solves by being one. The anti-magic room is the ultimate version (casters must find non-magical answers).

Pace in segments

Clear/cross a deck, bunker, rest, plan. Don't play every fight; vary tempo. Sandbox-in-a-box.

No PC death when a player is absent

Standing rule.

Eve of Ruin — "Vecna about to win" Late Arc IV

The published apocalypse: Vecna's Ritual of Remaking is near-complete and the crew is racing the eleventh hour. In the module, secrets are the ritual fuel, funneled from cults everywhere. Ours: the figure is the spell, the monoliths are its points, and the years of casting have simultaneously been stressing Convergence toward Fracture. The endgame is half spell-completion, half structural collapse — the takeover succeeds because the spell finishes and Continuity has been so weakened by the work that it can't hold its shape against him.

That's why he can succeed where countless others have failed at "remake reality" plans: it isn't just that he has the right spell. It's that the structural ground was already half-collapsed by the time he was ready to step in. The two attacks aren't redundant — they're how the impossible move becomes possible.

Status: the failure state of our campaign and possible literal endgame if the crew falls behind. Use: lift its stakes and set pieces; discard its teleport-rail structure. Our living-world architecture is the correction to exactly what people disliked about it. The Ascension World is a walkable preview of it.

Open question (don't decide now): is the campaign's true ending "prevent Eve of Ruin" or "survive Eve of Ruin"? Read the table near node 4.
