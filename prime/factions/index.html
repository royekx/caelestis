Factions & Characters

Who's who, and who they answer to. Every character belongs to a group; every group has a place in the world.

The Order of the All-Father

The principal organization most aware of the hidden structure of the Continuum, though even it does not possess complete knowledge. Interprets the influence of the All-Father and acts to preserve Continuity where possible. Recruits from many worlds and cultures; members include mystics, scholars, spelljammers, operatives, diplomats, ritual specialists, and planetary wardens.

About the Order

Strength: reach. Weakness: partial understanding — knows enough to matter, but not enough to control the system it serves. Its doctrine can drift toward dogma, triage, overreach, or strategic blindness.

The Order has two field arms: the Watchers (sanctioned) and Rogue Watchers (heterodox). Both belong under the Order, not beside it.

The Watchers

The Order's sanctioned field arm — its members who go out into the worlds. A known, respected partner organization to the Fleet. More structured and morally forward than the Fleet. Authority is divine-by-belief: they believe the All-Father charged them, and that gives them a priesthood's bearing.

About the Watchers

Function: observe, report on, and when necessary intervene in worlds approaching dangerous thresholds. Planetary guardians, but not the simplistic heroes-who-solve-every-problem version. Their task is preservation of Continuity, not universal rescue. That distinction is often painful.

Relationship to the Fleet: the Fleet routinely transports them. The two need each other — Watchers have the mandate and the moral clarity but rely on the Fleet for reach; the Fleet has the muscle and the deniability but no divine mission. The crew lives at that seam.

How they read in the field: more likely to be mistaken for singular operatives, strange protectors, dangerous outsiders, or rumors than recognized as members of a larger order. Most worlds do not know they exist.

Rogue Watchers

Watchers who do not follow the Order's protocols as closely as it would prefer. They operate on their own judgment — sometimes intervening where doctrine would not, sometimes failing to intervene where doctrine would expect, sometimes simply taking the work in a different direction than they were sent. A range, not a category. Some are nearly indistinguishable from sanctioned Watchers; some are openly heterodox.

About the Rogue Watchers

The Order tolerates them with varying degrees of patience depending on the individual and the era. A Rogue Watcher who saves a world by acting outside doctrine is celebrated quietly; one who violates a precept and creates downstream harm is censured. The line between principled disobedience and dangerous overreach is not always clear to either side.

They can appear as local protectors, unorthodox interventionists, or quietly excommunicated former colleagues of sanctioned Watchers. Most worlds that encounter one cannot tell the difference between a Watcher and a Rogue Watcher.

Caelestis Academy & Fleet

The crew's home. The Academy is the recruitment and training arm; the Fleet is the operational body that grew from it. Self-made and non-governmental — someone built this organization; no council of worlds chartered it. Authority is self-bestowed by reputation, not granted by any UN-of-planets. For the station itself, see Realms → Caelestis.

About the Academy & Fleet

For-hire and covert. Contracts are load-bearing, not a side hustle. Most worlds don't know Wildspace exists; the Fleet blends in, deals with the few locals who are read in, leaves without rewriting a civilization's reality.

The cadet wing is the Academy; graduates become Driftwardens — the in-world name for the operational Corps, the selective arm that runs actual missions.

The Bridge

The high command structure of the Academy and the wider operational apparatus around it. Members oversee policy, training, funding, logistics, security, and mission assignment. Not omniscient; not always united. Some understand more of the hidden cosmological stakes than others.

About the Bridge

The Bridge's internal tensions are useful. It is entirely plausible for one officer to treat a problem as ordinary criminal sabotage, another to suspect Watcher business, and a third to quietly realize a monolith event may be brewing.

The Spectrum

The mid-tier factions. None of them is Vecna; each is the ordinary face of the same impulse, which is exactly why Vecna can hide inside it. Mostly faceless for now — they gain named members as play surfaces them.

The Concordance

An ideological force concerned with legitimacy, coherence, and metaphysical orthodoxy. Where the Fracture Cult wants to create peaks and the Syndicate wants to profit from instability, the Concordance wants to define which version of reality is acceptable and then enforce that definition.

Members may appear as scholars, adjudicators, ritual authorities, doctrinal reformers, or institutional purists. They are dangerous because they do not merely want power — they want reality itself to conform to principle.

The Fracture Cult

Exists to create peak events. Members believe rupture, catastrophe, concentration of will, and symbolic climax can break stagnant reality open and reveal higher truth, power, or destiny. In practice they accelerate worlds toward Zenith conditions through engineered crises and symbolic acts intended to force history into a sharper shape.

The Archivists

Interfere with monoliths. Drawn to monolith sites, incomplete archives, corrupted records, and the preservation or redirection of resonant meaning. Depending on the cell, they may approach monoliths as sacred texts, exploitable instruments, historical treasures, or mechanisms that must be controlled before others can reach them.

Not equivalent to the Restorers. A more immediate and lower-order force: meddlers, scholars, thieves, and ritual operators who interfere with the archival process itself.

The Syndicate

Exploits instability. Less a single ideological movement than a durable criminal-commercial network that profits wherever order weakens. Traffics in information, transport, salvage, black-market relics, smuggling, mercenary arrangements, and access.

Does not need to believe in Vecna, Zeniths, or Continuity to be dangerous. It simply knows instability pays.

The Restorers

Hidden and independent. Not public knowledge. Do not work with the Watchers and should not function as a routine allied faction. Pursue their own agenda in secrecy.

About the Restorers

They believe that if they can locate the Core Monolith — which in their doctrine is called the Mother of the Universe — they may be able to restore worlds, civilizations, and histories that have been lost. This belief drives them toward monolith lore, ruined worlds, erased archives, ritual theory, and hidden pathways through the Continuum.

Relationship to the Watchers: Most Watchers do not know the Restorers exist. At most, the upper echelon of the Order or the Bridge may possess fragmentary intelligence, old warnings, or secondhand reports.

How they read in play: not allies. A competing hidden agenda moving toward the same cosmological pressure points the Watchers monitor. They should become legible as a third force over time, not as friends.

The Runekeepers

A hidden order on an unfindable world. Practitioners of runic transit — a discipline of marking celestial bodies in ways that attune the practitioner to that body, and through it, to the body's heart. The order is small, deliberate, and not publicly known. Their existence is rumor at best.

About the Runekeepers

Their homeworld defends itself by being unfindable — a ritual the order maintains keeps the world withdrawn from cosmological visibility. The world is real; it is not lost; it is simply not discoverable by ordinary means. Even an exiled Runekeeper, sent into the wider cosmos, is given no map and no name — only the remembered sensory fragments and cultural impressions by which they might recognize home again.

The order does not work with the Watchers, the Order, the Restorers, or any other public faction. Other practitioners of arcane transit (teleportation specialists, planar travelers) do not recognize what they do as the same kind of work — and they are right not to.

Wayfinders

Members of the order sent out into the wider cosmos for specific purposes — to learn, to recover, to witness, occasionally to bring something back. A Wayfinder carries a partial mark, the inscription their training has earned them, which grows as their understanding does.

About Wayfinders

A Wayfinder with a complete mark can attune to a celestial body via ritual and time, opening a transit route between attunement points. The mechanics:

Working assumption: a Wayfinder attunes their mark to a celestial body (a world, possibly a moon, edge cases unclear), and the attunement reaches the body's heart at the cosmological layer below. Transit happens at that layer, body-to-body, between attuned points. This bypasses Forbiddance because Forbiddance was specified against Weave-based teleportation and planar travel — runic transit is neither.

The deeper diagnostic: a Wayfinder can only attune to bodies that have a heart. A constructed object (Caelestis is the test case) is not a body in the cosmologically meaningful sense and has no heart to reach.

The Runekeeper's artifact

Working assumption: the Lunarfoot Runekeeper holds a Reach-residue artifact, likely Boundary-biased like the World Surveyor but expressing the Axiom operationally rather than as a reading — enforcement of the world's distinctness from being found rather than detection of it. The ritual that hides the homeworld uses this artifact. The Runekeeper dimming means the ritual is failing; if the line breaks entirely, the world surfaces, which would itself be a Zenith risk.

Bolder alternative reading: the artifact is something older than the Reach.

The structural rebuke

The Runekeepers, through formal discipline and the integrity of their order, do at small scale and legitimately what the network attempts at cosmological scale through grave-good extraction. The order's existence is a quiet rebuke to the whole network project — they are the right way to do what the network is doing wrong.

Beshaba — full notes

Maid of Misfortune. Lady Doom. Goddess of bad luck, misfortune, and random mischance — and Tymora's twin sister. Now heard by Sol; his Oath of Misfortune is live, and black lightning manifests. Beshaba speaks minimally to him: "Stand," "Again," "Continue."

Tone calibration: she does not deliver speeches. Her presence is the minimum word needed to keep him standing against erasure. Sol's thematic role is existence through endurance; Beshaba is the divine bet on him persisting.

Tymora — full notes

Lady Luck. Goddess of good fortune, skill, and victory — Beshaba's twin and opposite. The two are bound: where one is worshipped, the other's shadow falls. Aethris was a Tymora-devoted world — its dominant culture worshipped fortune and treated the Misfortuned as aberrations.

Not yet a present voice the way Beshaba is. She matters here as the backdrop of Sol's origin and as the structural opposite his patron defines herself against.

Astra — full notes

A sentient paracausal celestial body wounded by the Existence Axiom, expanding via Starchild tethers. Not a god in the traditional sense — a cosmic entity that functions like one. "Starchildren" is the locked proper term for people like Casey; their tethers extend Astra's reach and change the fundamental nature of those regions.

Astra is the source; Polly is the voice.

Polly — full notes

The familiar voice that reaches Casey. Heard in Mirt's wardrobe in V01 ("Go there") and at the Sky Dock rail in V02 as an unnamed presence. Casey recognizes her without seeing her.

Polly is Astra's proxy — bonded to Casey's brother. The connection has not been named openly in fiction; narrative refers to her as "a familiar presence" or "a familiar voice."

Zerathis — full notes

The spellcaster who gifted the wardrobe to Mirt. A prior Starchild, now consumed into the paracausal layer of Virenspace. Casey's predecessor along this path.

Open question: did Zerathis recruit other Starchildren — determining if Virenspace is a two- or three-plus overlap site.

External Powers

The Rock of Bral, merchant powers, mercane traders, private captains, local governments, and world-level authorities. Most do not understand the Prime Continuum in any meaningful sense. They still matter — they control lanes, supplies, politics, cover stories, contracts, rumors, and access.

About External Powers

Treat them as practical powers rather than cosmological experts. They are often the reason missions happen at all.

Unaffiliated
