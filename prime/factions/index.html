Forces & People

Factions & Characters

Who's who, and who they answer to. Every character belongs to a group; every group has a place in the world. Cosmology says what is true; Realms says where it lives; this is who's moving through it. Operational state — where someone is this voyage, what they want right now — lives in the Campaign Tracker; durable character lives here.

Running This Page

Organized as groups with members. Each group leads with what the faction is, then lists the people who belong to it. To find someone, think about who they answer to. To find a force, read its lead entry and ignore the roster.

Setting vs. campaign Most of this page is setting — it exists regardless of which campaign runs here. The Order, the Academy & Fleet, the mid-tier factions, the gods: all reusable for a one-shot or a different crew. Entries tied to this campaign specifically (the network's current agents, the cadets bound to this crew, the patrons of these PCs) carry a This campaign tag. When you reuse the setting, keep the untagged; reset the tagged.
The spectrum of the same sin The mid-tier factions are each an ordinary answer to the campaign's question — who decides what's real? That's why the antagonist can hide inside them: The Remaking isn't a new kind of evil, just the same impulse pushed to cosmic scale. Read each faction's one-line verdict as a variation on the theme.

The Order of the All-Father

The principal organization most aware of the hidden structure of the Continuum, though even it does not possess complete knowledge. The Order interprets the influence of the All-Father and acts to preserve Continuity where possible. Recruits from many worlds and cultures. Members include mystics, scholars, spelljammers, operatives, diplomats, ritual specialists, and planetary wardens.

Strength
Reach.
Weakness
Partial understanding. Knows enough to matter, but not enough to control the system it serves. Its doctrine can drift toward dogma, triage, overreach, or strategic blindness.
The hierarchy The Watchers are the Order's field arm — its members who go out to the worlds. Rogue Watchers are that same field arm corrupted. Both belong under the Order, not beside it.

The Watchers The Order's field arm

The Order's members who go out into the worlds — its hidden field wardens. A known, respected partner organization to the Fleet. More structured and more morally forward than the Fleet. Authority is divine-by-belief — they believe the All-Father charged them. That gives them a priesthood's bearing: the right-and-wrong of it, the when-to-intervene gravity, the willingness to make hard calls about which worlds continue.

Function
Observe, report on, and when necessary intervene in worlds approaching dangerous thresholds. Planetary guardians, but not the simplistic heroes-who-solve-every-problem version. Their task is preservation of Continuity, not universal rescue. That distinction is often painful.
Relationship to the Fleet
The Fleet routinely transports them. The two need each other: the Watchers have the mandate and the moral clarity but rely on the Fleet for reach. The Fleet has the muscle and the deniability but no divine mission. The crew lives at that seam.
Their internals
Discreet, not secret. Their inner workings aren't public — ordinary institutional discretion, not mystique. The Fleet knows about them. Most worlds do not.
When noticed in the field
They are more likely to be mistaken for singular operatives, strange protectors, dangerous outsiders, or rumors than recognized as members of a larger order.
Recalibrated from prior canon The Watchers are not mysterious or ominous to the Fleet. Earlier framing leaned hard on hidden-cipher energy — that's been retired. They are a known, principled working partner. Mystery only reads from outside the Fleet (random worlds in the field don't know them).
Ezra Watcher-in-training This campaign

A Watcher-in-training. The Foundling's errand is to collect him for a rendezvous and in-field training at H'Catha; he attaches to the crew for that mission. A known quantity to the Fleet, riding along to learn the job — not a cipher.

Crossroads breadcrumb: tie to a PC from one of your other campaigns. Player Easter egg.

Rogue Watchers The field arm corrupted

A Watcher who has lost proportion, restraint, or trust in organic history. Such a figure may believe people, cultures, and worlds must be controlled more aggressively for their own protection.

The danger is not ignorance, but partial knowledge fused to certainty. They can become local antagonists who justify severe intervention in the name of preventing something worse. The most principled organization is exactly the one whose certainty can curdle.

Their answer to who decides what's real: I do — I decide who survives, for their own good.

Caelestis Academy & Fleet

The crew's home. The Academy is the recruitment and training arm; the Fleet is the operational body that grew from it. Self-made and non-governmental — someone built this organization; no council of worlds chartered it. Authority is self-bestowed by reputation, not granted by any UN-of-planets.

For-hire and covert. Contracts are load-bearing, not a side hustle. Most worlds don't know Wildspace exists; the Fleet blends in, deals with the few locals who are read in, leaves without rewriting a civilization's reality. Built-in constraint: fix the problem without revealing the sky.

The cadet wing is the Academy; graduates become Driftwardens — the in-world name for the operational Corps, the selective arm that runs actual missions. Full operational structure: Campaign → Arc II Structure. The station itself is at Realms → Caelestis.

The Bridge High command

The high command structure of the Academy and the wider operational apparatus around it. Members oversee policy, training, funding, logistics, security, and mission assignment. Not omniscient. Not always united. Some understand more of the hidden cosmological stakes than others.

Campaign fuel The Bridge's internal tensions are useful. It is entirely plausible for one officer to treat a problem as ordinary criminal sabotage, another to suspect Watcher business, and a third to quietly realize a monolith event may be brewing. Use this when assignments come down.

The Network

The antagonist's agents in play — the human (and not-so-human) face of The Remaking. No institution knows these figures are one coherent operation; the crew assembles that before anyone. For the plan itself, see The Remaking → The Network.

Vocath Plan locked This campaign

A mercane (interstellar merchant). His grudge against Mirt is commercial and personal, through the school. He doesn't need to kill Mirt or destroy the school — he needs to disgrace Mirt enough to prise him loose from his protection so someone else finishes him.

The thefts and sabotage are manufactured noise — cover for the chart heist running quiet underneath, and a campaign to wreck Mirt's standing. Vocath happily owns the grudge because it's perfect cover. Not a cultist. The network is the hidden hand using his operation. Full detail at The Remaking → The Network.

Qitru This campaign

Vocath's intermediary (or her proxy). The voice on the other end of Miken's hushed magical contact. "You had an agreement. You have to do this."

In the module, Vocath's people hunt Miken to retrieve him. Reframed: the network wants Miken silenced — he's a burned, one-use asset who can identify the intermediary/disc. Qitru's job is cleanup. Future hook.

The Crystalline Retrieval Construct (CRC) Sol's antagonist This campaign

Built by the Fortuned on Aethris after Sol's escape — his escape was the trigger, not a predicted contingency. Mission: capture, correction, return. Not murder.

Signature line: "Sol Fortuna. You are required."

Tone: cold cosmic inevitability, sterile certainty, beautiful-yet-wrong, no taunts or posturing. Inspirations: T-1000, Amazo, Cell, cosmic construct. First encounter on Caelestis is an objective-based escape (not a kill). Recurring nemesis; thematic foil to Sol's question "Am I a person or a function?"

Open: who on Aethris cared enough to commission it, and why. Pending

Not the network, but adjacent The CRC isn't part of Vecna's network — it's the Fortuned's own machine, pursuing Sol for reasons local to Aethris. It sits here because it's an antagonist agent the crew will treat as a threat, but it answers to a different master. Keep that distinction; it matters when the crew tries to understand who's hunting them.

The Spectrum

The mid-tier factions — the spectrum of the same sin. None of them is Vecna; each is the ordinary face of the same impulse, which is exactly why Vecna can hide inside it. Each answers who decides what's real? a different way. Mostly faceless for now — they gain named members as play surfaces them.

The Concordance Mid-tier

An ideological force concerned with legitimacy, coherence, and metaphysical orthodoxy. Where the Fracture Cult wants to create peaks and the Syndicate wants to profit from instability, the Concordance wants to define which version of reality is acceptable and then enforce that definition.

Members may appear as scholars, adjudicators, ritual authorities, doctrinal reformers, or institutional purists. They are dangerous because they do not merely want power. They want reality itself to conform to principle.

Their answer: we do — we enforce which reality is legitimate.

The Fracture Cult Mid-tier

Exists to create peak events. Members believe rupture, catastrophe, concentration of will, and symbolic climax can break stagnant reality open and reveal higher truth, power, or destiny. In practice, they accelerate worlds toward Zenith conditions by fomenting rituals, uprisings, engineered crises, sacrificial turning points, and symbolic acts intended to force history into a sharper shape.

Their answer: we do — we force meaning by breaking the world into a sharper shape.

The Archivists Mid-tier

Interfere with monoliths. Drawn to monolith sites, incomplete archives, corrupted records, and the preservation or redirection of resonant meaning. Depending on the cell, they may approach monoliths as sacred texts, exploitable instruments, historical treasures, or mechanisms that must be controlled before others can reach them.

Not equivalent to the Restorers. A more immediate and lower-order force: meddlers, scholars, thieves, ritual operators, and handlers of monolith knowledge who interfere with the archival process itself.

Their answer: we do — we hoard what's allowed to be remembered.

The Syndicate Mid-tier

Exploits instability. Less a single ideological movement than a durable criminal-commercial network that profits wherever order weakens. When crises scatter people, sever supply lines, expose relics, loosen institutional oversight, or make strange cargo move quietly, the Syndicate appears.

Traffics in information, transport, salvage, black-market relics, smuggling, mercenary arrangements, and access. Does not need to believe in Vecna, Zeniths, or Continuity to be dangerous. It simply knows instability pays.

Their answer: nobody, and that's profitable — beings are cargo.

The Restorers Hidden faction

Hidden and independent. Not public knowledge. Do not work with the Watchers and should not function as a routine allied faction for the party. Pursue their own agenda in secrecy and should remain unknown until the campaign deliberately reveals them (Arc III).

They believe that if they can locate the Core Monolith — which in their doctrine is called the Mother of the Universe — they may be able to restore worlds, civilizations, and histories that have been lost. This belief drives them toward monolith lore, ruined worlds, erased archives, ritual theory, and hidden pathways through the Continuum.

Relationship to the Watchers
Most Watchers do not know the Restorers exist. At most, the upper echelon of the Order or the Bridge may possess fragmentary intelligence, old warnings, or secondhand reports, but direct knowledge is scarce and encounters are rarer still.
How they read in play
Not allies. A competing hidden agenda moving toward the same cosmological pressure points the Watchers monitor. By Arc III, they should become legible as a third force, not as friends.

The Runekeepers Hidden order Working name

A hidden order on an unfindable world. Practitioners of runic transit — a discipline of marking celestial bodies in ways that attune the practitioner to that body, and through it, to the body's heart. Through this attunement, a Runekeeper can move between attuned bodies via the cosmological layer the marks reach into. The order is small, deliberate, and not publicly known.

Their homeworld defends itself by being unfindable — a ritual the order maintains keeps the world withdrawn from cosmological visibility. The world is real; it is not lost; it is simply not discoverable by ordinary means. Even an exiled Runekeeper, sent into the wider cosmos, is given no map and no name — only the remembered sensory fragments and cultural impressions by which they might recognize home again.

The order does not work with the Watchers, the Order, the Restorers, or any other public faction. Their existence is rumor at best. Other practitioners of arcane transit (teleportation specialists, planar travelers) do not recognize what they do as the same kind of work — and they are right not to.

Wayfinders Field arm

Members of the order sent out into the wider cosmos for specific purposes — to learn, to recover, to witness, occasionally to bring something back. A Wayfinder carries a partial mark, the inscription their training has earned them, which grows as their understanding does. A Wayfinder with a complete mark can attune to a celestial body via ritual and time, opening a transit route between attunement points.

Members in play
Tumak (Wan & Tu) — twin halflings disguised as one person, both partial-Wayfinders. The current Lunarfoot Runekeeper is dimming; both twins were sent as a hedge, accepting the Zenith risk a simultaneous twin return carries. See Realms → Tumak's homeworld for the world entry.
Mechanics of the Wayfinder mark — resolution pending Working assumption: a Wayfinder attunes their mark to a celestial body (a world, possibly a moon, edge cases unclear), and the attunement reaches the body's heart at the cosmological layer below. Transit happens at that layer, body-to-body, between attuned points. This bypasses Forbiddance because Forbiddance was specified against Weave-based teleportation and planar travel — runic transit is neither. The deeper diagnostic: a Wayfinder can only attune to bodies that have a heart. A constructed object (Caelestis is the test case) is not a body in the cosmologically meaningful sense and has no heart to reach — runic transit fails not because of the wards but because there's nothing to attune to. To resolve before Tumak has his full mark and the question becomes live at the table.
The Runekeeper's artifact — resolution pending Working assumption: the Lunarfoot Runekeeper holds a Reach-residue artifact, likely Boundary-biased like the World Surveyor but expressing the Axiom operationally rather than as a reading — enforcement of the world's distinctness from being found rather than detection of it. The ritual that hides the homeworld uses this artifact. The Runekeeper dimming means the ritual is failing; if the line breaks entirely, the world surfaces, which would itself be a Zenith risk. Bolder alternative reading: the artifact is something older than the Reach. To resolve when Tumak's homeworld is approached at the table.
The structural rebuke The Runekeepers, through formal discipline and the integrity of their order, do at small scale and legitimately what the network attempts at cosmological scale through grave-good extraction. The order's existence is a quiet rebuke to the whole network project — they are the right way to do what the network is doing wrong. If they ever enter the campaign properly, this is a major political question, not just an alliance question.

Deities & Cosmic Powers

The divine and the cosmic-but-not-divine — gods, and the paracausal powers that function like them. For what gods are in this cosmology and the bounds of what they know, see Cosmology → The Gods; this is the roster of the ones who matter here.

Beshaba Sol's patron This campaign

Maid of Misfortune. Lady Doom. Goddess of bad luck, misfortune, and random mischance — and Tymora's twin sister. Now heard by Sol; his Oath of Misfortune is live, and black lightning manifests. Beshaba speaks minimally to him: "Stand," "Again," "Continue."

Tone calibration: she does not deliver speeches. Her presence is the minimum word needed to keep him standing against erasure. Sol's thematic role is existence through endurance; Beshaba is the divine bet on him persisting.

Tymora Lady Luck

Lady Luck. Goddess of good fortune, skill, and victory — Beshaba's twin and opposite. The two are bound: where one is worshipped, the other's shadow falls. Aethris was a Tymora-devoted world — its dominant culture worshipped fortune and treated the Misfortuned as aberrations, which is the soil Sol was born wrong into.

Not yet a present voice the way Beshaba is. She matters here as the backdrop of Sol's origin — the faith his world organized itself around — and as the structural opposite his patron defines herself against. Develop if Sol's homeworld surfaces.

Astra Wounded celestial This campaign

A sentient paracausal celestial body wounded by the Existence Axiom, expanding via Starchild tethers. Not a god in the traditional sense — a cosmic entity that functions like one. "Starchildren" is the locked proper term for people like Casey; their tethers extend Astra's reach and change the fundamental nature of those regions.

See Campaign → The Weather of Reality for Astra's widening reach as a non-Vecna cosmic pull. Astra is the source; Polly is the voice.

Polly Casey's patron This campaign

The familiar voice that reaches Casey. Heard in Mirt's wardrobe in V01 ("Go there") and at the Sky Dock rail in V02 as an unnamed presence. Casey recognizes her without seeing her.

Polly is Astra's proxy — bonded to Casey's brother. The connection has not been named openly in fiction; narrative refers to her as "a familiar presence" or "a familiar voice."

Zerathis Prior Starchild This campaign

The spellcaster who gifted the wardrobe to Mirt. A prior Starchild, now consumed into the paracausal layer of Virenspace. Casey's predecessor along this path. Open question: did Zerathis recruit other Starchildren — determining if Virenspace is a two- or three-plus overlap site.

External Powers

The Rock of Bral, merchant powers, mercane traders, private captains, local governments, and world-level authorities all interact with the Fleet and the Order to varying degrees. Most do not understand the Prime Continuum in any meaningful sense. They still matter. They control lanes, supplies, politics, cover stories, contracts, rumors, and access.

Treat them as practical powers rather than cosmological experts. They are often the reason missions happen at all.
