Campaign

The DM's working manual for this wiki. How the site is organized, what each page does, and the operating principles for running Caelestis. For the campaign's story content — acts, the crew, where the campaign is going — see The Spine.

Wiki structure

Two domains: setting (the universe, reusable across any campaign run here) and campaign (this run specifically). A new entry must belong to one, not both. When they overlap — an artifact held at a station, a faction with named members — the setting page holds the durable object and the campaign page holds the current state.

The setting / campaign distinction

Setting = facts about the universe that exist regardless of which campaign runs in it. The Continuum, the All-Father, monoliths, Caelestis-the-station, the Order of the All-Father, Beshaba as a deity, the World Surveyor as an artifact. Other DMs running in this universe should be able to reuse these.

Campaign = facts about this run: who Vecna's current network is, who the PCs are, what just happened in the last voyage, the Brig heist plan, the current state of the Fork. Restart a campaign and most of this is reset.

The simplest test: if a one-shot DM in this universe would have to delete content from a page to reuse it, the content is on the wrong page.

What each page does

Two clusters: the four reference pillars (setting) and the campaign documents.

Setting — the four reference pillars
Cosmology
What is true about the universe. The Prime Continuum, Continuity, the Axioms, paracausality, Zenith events, monoliths, the Core, the Gods, the Reach. Principles and structural facts.
Realms
Where things are. Geography organized Wildspace → celestial body → locale. Plus an Untethered section for places not yet placed in a sphere, and a Things in Play section for named objects that don't yet warrant their own setting entry.
Factions & Characters
Who's who. Organized as groups with members — each faction's lead describes the faction; the people who belong to it appear in a dossier table below, with deeper detail in collapsibles.
Artifacts
Named objects of cosmological weight. Each entry is the artifact-as-object: physical description, mechanical function, cosmological origin, general properties, set context, ethics, current-custody status.
Campaign — this run's documents
The Spine
The campaign's authored shape. Acts, the crew, story direction, beats sketched, what each act is becoming. Where the DM prep work for the campaign-shape happens.
Voyages
Per-session record. Each entry compares the voyage briefing (planned) against the voyage (actual), with a Delta section documenting what changed. The audit surface that feeds back into Spine planning.
The Remaking
Antagonist throughline. Vecna, the network, the inscription, the figure, the current state of the operation.
Plot Hooks
Grounded threads — hooks with foundations already in the wiki, pullable on demand. Tagged by type (character / place / event / object).
What If
Untethered brainstorm. Cosmological speculation, late-arc levers, ideas that may or may not enter the world. When a What If gets grounded, it graduates to Plot Hooks.

Operating principles

The rules that hold the campaign together. Short list; each is a working commitment rather than a description.

No-railroad — players bend tempo, not direction

The cosmic plot continues regardless of what the players do, but how fast and how cleanly it advances depends on their choices. Every major outcome resolves into multiple states, all of which feed forward. The graduation Fork is the canonical example.

What players never get: the ability to hard-stop the plot by holding a key item. What they always get: the ability to make the antagonist work harder, take losses, change tactics, and miss windows.

The Convergence Rule — one strain, many expressions

Stress on Continuity at cosmological scale shows up at every smaller scale too. When the wider cosmos is straining (because of the Remaking), so are individual worlds, factions, NPCs, and PCs. Paracausal events become more common; gods stretch their portfolios; cosmic powers reach further than they should.

Practical implication: a strange event on a world is rarely just that world's problem. It's usually a local expression of a cosmic stress that's present everywhere — and that stress will surface in the PCs' arcs too. Paracausality is the most common local expression.

The Crossroads Principle — sit on the seam

Caelestis is published-canon Spelljammer Academy, running through Realmspace, which is published-canon Forgotten Realms. This campaign lives on the seam between the published worlds and a homebrew cosmology. Treat the published material as backdrop and junction rather than current destination; surface canon references as breadcrumbs (Toril, Mirt, Saerthe, Bral) rather than as the main story.

The principle has three concrete moves: (1) name canonical things accurately when they appear; (2) don't make the campaign about canon resolution; (3) use the published world as the contrast that makes the homebrew weight matter.

The give-and-take rule — hooks are directions, not scripts

A hook's "if pulled" is a direction, not a script. If players pull it, that direction becomes real and develops. If they pull it somewhere unexpected, bend the destination — but keep the route back to the spine. No hook is wasted; unpulled hooks stay planted for later.

Iconic items, recontextualized

Famous D&D items exist in this multiverse — the Hand and Eye of Vecna, the Book of Vile Darkness, others — but their fame was earned in a different era of their associated beings. Vecna's Hand and Eye, for example, are artifacts of his god-of-secrets phase, long before he stepped back far enough to see the machinery beneath gods. They are not part of The Remaking; they would not help anyone advance or stop it. They are period pieces.

The campaign principle this implies: recognized power is suspicious here. Real power tends to wear a different costume. The artifacts that matter to the current crisis are mostly things nobody has named — or things named so flatly that the names don't carry weight. When iconic items appear, they appear as era-appropriate background, not as the right kind of clue.

When to bring one in: if the iconic item would say something — about a being's earlier project, about the Fracture Cult's relic-hunger, about what a god-of-secrets thought a useful tool looked like — it can show up. If it would just be a fan-service cameo, leave it out. The test is whether the item slots into the Continuum's logic, not D&D's.

The teleport-seal — physical movement matters

Fleet installations of consequence are warded against teleportation via permanent Forbiddance. Authorized internal transit uses bound Teleportation Circle sigils issued to specific personnel. For Caelestis specifically, see Realms → Caelestis Security.

Campaign implication: physical movement is the default for any operation against a hardened target. Infiltrators can't teleport in; they arrive as someone. They can't teleport out; they leave on a ship. Vecna himself can't teleport to monoliths — he must reach them patiently, through agents. Months of reconnaissance become load-bearing because the wards remove every shortcut.

Hook taxonomy

Five kinds of hooks land at the table; each gets pulled in a different way. Use this to identify what kind of hook you're planting and how the crew can encounter it.

The five hook types
Character
Encountered through a person. An NPC who knows something, wants something, or is involved in something. The NPC is the door.
Place
Encountered by going somewhere. The location holds the hook; arriving triggers it. The place is the door.
Event
Encountered by an incident happening. The crew is present for something — an attack, an arrival, a discovery, a death — and the event opens the thread.
Object
Encountered through a thing in hand. An item that hums, a record that says too much, a sigil they can't identify. The object is the door.
Pattern
Encountered by noticing connections. Multiple smaller incidents that together imply something larger. The pattern is the door, and only the crew can recognize it.

Most hooks combine types. The H'Catha meteor is object (the meteor) and event (the strike). The Latchling is place (Sea Dock), object (the thing itself), and character (Rindle as obstacle). Tagging by all relevant types in Plot Hooks makes the hook surface from multiple angles.

Wiki conventions

Patterns used consistently across the site. New entries should match these unless there's a deliberate reason to break.

Page structure

Every page has: page header with eyebrow + title + lede, then h2 sections, optional h3 subsections, optional collapsible detail. Setting pages don't carry "Running the X" preambles — those have moved here.

Headings are declarative, not interrogative. No questions as section titles.

The dossier pattern

For pages that catalog entities (people in Factions, objects in Artifacts), the visible default is a dossier table showing each entity's portrait, name, role, and brief detail. Deeper content lives in collapsible sections below the table.

Portraits live in scripts/portraits.js — a centralized map of character keys to Google Drive file IDs. Rows reference the key; the rendering helper fills in the image or a monogram placeholder.

Collapsibles

Anything multi-paragraph that lives under a heading should be collapsible (<details class="dm-collapse">) by default. The reader sees the heading and a brief identity statement; they expand what they want detail on. Walls of text are the failure mode.

The exception: introductory ledes and short identity paragraphs immediately under a heading. Those stay visible because they're what tells the reader what the section is.

Tags

Tags appear inside headings or card titles. They communicate cosmological or structural state at a glance — what kind of thing the entry is, whether it's locked, whether it's open. Tags do not appear in the right-rail TOC (the script strips them when reading heading text).

Campaign-state tags (Home base, Confirmed, Active, In the Brig) do not belong on setting pages because the state may change with the campaign. They go on campaign pages instead. Setting pages use only structural tags (is-cosmic, is-stub, is-open for genuine resolution-pending).

Cross-references

Setting pages cross-reference each other freely. Campaign pages reference setting pages, never the reverse — with one narrow exception: an artifact's current custody field can reference Remaking, because custody is genuinely current-state metadata.

Use relative paths. From prime/artifacts/index.html to the Brig: ../realms/#brig. From prime/campaign/remaking/index.html to Artifacts: ../../artifacts/#world-surveyor. The double-up is correct — Remaking is one level deeper than its siblings.

Building new entries

Quick decision rule when adding something to the wiki. Answer in order:

  1. Is this a cosmological principle? → Cosmology
  2. Is this a place? → Realms
  3. Is this a person, group, or faction? → Factions & Characters
  4. Is this a named object of cosmological weight? → Artifacts
  5. Is this story shape or per-act planning? → The Spine
  6. Is this antagonist throughline? → The Remaking
  7. Is this a pullable thread grounded in the wiki? → Plot Hooks
  8. Is this untethered speculation? → What If
  9. Did this just happen at the table? → Voyages

If content fits two places, one is wrong. The setting/campaign boundary is usually what's at issue.
