Caelestis

The station. Home base. The campaign's broader headquarters and long-term operational base. A strategic platform and a symbolic one: the place where military readiness, exploration, cosmological mystery, and interworld obligation meet.

Lore Bible correction The Citadel is not close to Caelestis. The prior framing has been retired — see Physics → The Core & the Citadel. Caelestis is a strategic node on its own merits, not a Citadel satellite.

Structure — one station, two functions

Caelestis is a single station containing two distinct operational wings:

The Academy wing

Mirt's domain. Training, cadets, simulation deck, classrooms, the Spelljammer Nexus, the Weeping Goddess refectory. Where the crew came up. The cadet life ends when the crew graduates.

The Fleet operations wing

The handler's domain. The contract board, the working docks, visiting captains. Part base, part market. The space-bar-adjacent social layer lives here. Graduation = crossing the station from school side to operations side. The threshold is internal — not relocating, just earning the doors.

Realmspace

Toril's Wildspace. The crew's current operating area (the geographic leash — see Campaign → Scope). The published canonical material for this sphere stands as backdrop; this campaign uses it as a junction more than a destination — see the Crossroads Principle.

Travel within Realmspace is fast — days between worlds via spelljamming helm. Between Realmspace and the rest of the Continuum is the phlogiston, which is reserved for senior-crew long-haul work (the rank-buys-range progression).

The Anchor Worlds

Two reachable worlds that show the crew their own future. Together they are the two faces of the warning — and why the crew fights.

Aethris — a completed manufactured Zenith Cosmic anchor

Sol's homeworld. A completed Zenith — one finished glyph. Someone forced this world past its threshold and the working succeeded. The Prism Ascendant still stands as the architecture of that success. Sol is the one survivor, the loose end.

Standing here is standing inside what other worlds are being aimed toward. This is someone else deciding what's real, made permanent at a planetary scale.

The Misfortuned
The category Sol was born into on Aethris — those whom the world's dominant Tymora-worship considered aberrations. A failed Zenith ritual was the trigger of his escape; his survival is the world's loose end.
The Fortuned
Sol's counterparts. The fortune-blessed Aethris elite. They commissioned the Crystalline Retrieval Construct after Sol's escape, to bring him back.
The Prism Ascendant
The architecture of the Zenith. Still standing. Function unclear at this scale; one of the things Aethris-as-destination will eventually reveal.

The Ascension World Specifics pending Cosmic anchor

A reachable world where Vecna once forced a threshold and won, locally — a small, personal ascension (not the canonical Sigil event) that left the world a scar: where "who decides what's real" was decided permanently in one being's favor and everything else became a footnote to him.

Standing on it is standing inside the failure state — a local Eve of Ruin you can walk. This is Vecna deciding. Together with Aethris, it's the campaign's thesis made into ground the crew can stand on.

Build later Canon permits this — Vecna's ascension origins are deliberately diffuse, so we're filling a gap, not contradicting one. Hold specifics for Arc II-late / III. Should be namable on the charts now as an unmarked point that resonates wrong. Decide name and what the local ascension did when the crew is approaching it.

Tumak's homeworld Unnamed by design

Hides itself cosmically. The world is a living thing that decides what reaches it and what does not. Tumak does not know its name — this is intentional and cultural, not a gap. The name is withheld from the exiled child to preserve the integrity of the journey. Tumak must identify home through remembered sensory fragments and cultural impressions rather than by name or direct coordinates.

The world's concealment is maintained by the Runekeeper, whose body bears the inscription. The current Lunarfoot Runekeeper is dimming, which is why Wan and Tu were both sent as Wayfinders — a hedge against catastrophe that creates its own Zenith risk if both return together. Convergence + Intensification at personal scale.

Full mechanical detail in the Wayfinder/Runekeeper mechanics doc. See also: the Tumak / The Becoming seed.

Cairn Station Open thread

A station the artifact's trail eventually leads to. Sits in the territory of an astral dreadnought — the apex predator of the Astral Sea, a titanic one-eyed chain-tailed leviathan that hunts the dead matter of the cosmos.

The dreadnought is the lock, not the boss

The crew does not fight the dreadnought. It's the environmental pressure that makes Cairn Station a white-knuckle destination — you avoid it, slip past it, time your movement to its patrol, get in and out before the eye turns your way.

Why information is hidden there
If you want to stash a piece of a cosmos-ending working where no institution will stumble on it, you put it in a dreadnought's hunting ground. The monster is the lock. Nobody audits a place that eats auditors. "Cairn" reads right — a cairn is a marker over the dead, and this is a station perched in a boneyard.
Why it's thematically loaded
An astral dreadnought eats the dead matter of the cosmos — it's the Astral's mechanism for unmaking what's already ended. A god-corpse-eater squatting over the network's secret is the charter's question wearing teeth: who decides what's real? The thing that eats what's already been decided-against.
Future-seed (don't decide yet)
The dreadnought's presence at Cairn may not be coincidence. Something about the network's working may attract these creatures, or the network may use the territory deliberately as a vault. Don't decide now; the resonance is there if needed.

Open question — what is Cairn Station, in character?

Decide when approached Two clean directions:
  • A) Derelict / dead site. A literal cairn — an abandoned archive or wreck in the boneyard. The network goes there to retrieve the next chart-piece from a place no one guards because the dreadnought does it for free. Heist-against-environment.
  • B) Inhabited. A hidden outpost of fences/scholars, existing in the dreadnought's shadow precisely because that keeps the authorities away. The network has to deal with them to get the next piece. Gives you a recurring location and NPCs.

Factions

Forces in play, ordered roughly from most-aware-of-the-cosmology to most-incidental. The mid-tier factions form the spectrum of the same sin — each a different answer to who decides what's real.

The Order of the All-Father Allied

The principal organization most aware of the hidden structure of the Continuum, though even it does not possess complete knowledge. The Order interprets the influence of the All-Father and acts to preserve Continuity where possible. Recruits from many worlds and cultures. Members include mystics, scholars, spelljammers, operatives, diplomats, ritual specialists, and planetary wardens.

Strength
Reach.
Weakness
Partial understanding. Knows enough to matter, but not enough to control the system it serves. Its doctrine can drift toward dogma, triage, overreach, or strategic blindness.
The Watchers Known partner

A known, respected partner organization. More structured and more morally forward than the Fleet. Authority is divine-by-belief — they believe the All-Father charged them. That gives them a priesthood's bearing: the right-and-wrong of it, the when-to-intervene gravity, the willingness to make hard calls about which worlds continue.

Function
The Order's hidden field wardens. Observe, report on, and when necessary intervene in worlds approaching dangerous thresholds. Planetary guardians, but not the simplistic heroes-who-solve-every-problem version. Their task is preservation of Continuity, not universal rescue. That distinction is often painful.
Relationship to the Fleet
The Fleet routinely transports them. The two need each other: the Watchers have the mandate and the moral clarity but rely on the Fleet for reach. The Fleet has the muscle and the deniability but no divine mission. The crew lives at that seam.
Their internals
Discreet, not secret. Their inner workings aren't public — ordinary institutional discretion, not mystique. The Fleet knows about them. Most worlds do not.
When noticed in the field
They are more likely to be mistaken for singular operatives, strange protectors, dangerous outsiders, or rumors than recognized as members of a larger order.
Recalibrated from prior canon The Watchers are not mysterious or ominous to the Fleet. Earlier framing leaned hard on hidden-cipher energy — that's been retired. They are a known, principled working partner. Mystery only reads from outside the Fleet (random worlds in the field don't know them).
The Restorers Hidden faction

Hidden and independent. Not public knowledge. Do not work with the Watchers and should not function as a routine allied faction for the party. Pursue their own agenda in secrecy and should remain unknown until the campaign deliberately reveals them (Arc III).

They believe that if they can locate the Core Monolith — which in their doctrine is called the Mother of the Universe — they may be able to restore worlds, civilizations, and histories that have been lost. This belief drives them toward monolith lore, ruined worlds, erased archives, ritual theory, and hidden pathways through the Continuum.

Relationship to the Watchers
Most Watchers do not know the Restorers exist. At most, the upper echelon of the Order or the Bridge may possess fragmentary intelligence, old warnings, or secondhand reports, but direct knowledge is scarce and encounters are rarer still.
How they read in play
Not allies. A competing hidden agenda moving toward the same cosmological pressure points the Watchers monitor. By Arc III, they should become legible as a third force, not as friends.
The Bridge High command

The high command structure of the Academy and the wider operational apparatus around it. Members oversee policy, training, funding, logistics, security, and mission assignment. Not omniscient. Not always united. Some understand more of the hidden cosmological stakes than others.

Campaign fuel The Bridge's internal tensions are useful campaign fuel. It is entirely plausible for one officer to treat a problem as ordinary criminal sabotage, another to suspect Watcher business, and a third to quietly realize a monolith event may be brewing. Use this when assignments come down.
The Spelljammer Academy & the Fleet The crew's home

The Academy is the recruitment and training arm; the Fleet is the operational body that grew from it. Self-made and non-governmental — someone built this organization; no council of worlds chartered it. Authority is self-bestowed by reputation, not granted by any UN-of-planets.

For-hire and covert. Contracts are load-bearing, not a side hustle. Most worlds don't know Wildspace exists; the Fleet blends in, deals with the few locals who are read in, leaves without rewriting a civilization's reality. Built-in constraint: fix the problem without revealing the sky.

The cadet wing of this organization is the Academy; graduates become Driftwardens — the in-world name for the operational Corps, the selective arm that runs actual missions.

Full operational structure: Campaign → Arc II Structure.

The Concordance Mid-tier

An ideological force concerned with legitimacy, coherence, and metaphysical orthodoxy. Where the Fracture Cult wants to create peaks and the Syndicate wants to profit from instability, the Concordance wants to define which version of reality is acceptable and then enforce that definition.

Members may appear as scholars, adjudicators, ritual authorities, doctrinal reformers, or institutional purists. They are dangerous because they do not merely want power. They want reality itself to conform to principle.

Their answer to who decides what's real: we do — we enforce which reality is legitimate.

The Fracture Cult Mid-tier

Exists to create peak events. Members believe rupture, catastrophe, concentration of will, and symbolic climax can break stagnant reality open and reveal higher truth, power, or destiny. In practice, they accelerate worlds toward Zenith conditions by fomenting rituals, uprisings, engineered crises, sacrificial turning points, and symbolic acts intended to force history into a sharper shape.

Their answer to who decides what's real: we do — we force meaning by breaking the world into a sharper shape.

The Archivists Mid-tier

Interfere with monoliths. Drawn to monolith sites, incomplete archives, corrupted records, and the preservation or redirection of resonant meaning. Depending on the cell, they may approach monoliths as sacred texts, exploitable instruments, historical treasures, or mechanisms that must be controlled before others can reach them.

Not equivalent to the Restorers. A more immediate and lower-order force: meddlers, scholars, thieves, ritual operators, and handlers of monolith knowledge who interfere with the archival process itself.

Their answer to who decides what's real: we do — we hoard what's allowed to be remembered.

The Syndicate Mid-tier

Exploits instability. Less a single ideological movement than a durable criminal-commercial network that profits wherever order weakens. When crises scatter people, sever supply lines, expose relics, loosen institutional oversight, or make strange cargo move quietly, the Syndicate appears.

Traffics in information, transport, salvage, black-market relics, smuggling, mercenary arrangements, and access. Does not need to believe in Vecna, Zeniths, or Continuity to be dangerous. It simply knows instability pays.

Their answer to who decides what's real: nobody, and that's profitable — beings are cargo.

Rogue Watchers Recurring threat

Not a faction in the same sense as the others, but an important recurring category of threat. A Rogue Watcher is someone shaped by Watcher logic who has lost proportion, restraint, or trust in organic history. Such a figure may believe people, cultures, and worlds must be controlled more aggressively for their own protection.

The danger is not ignorance, but partial knowledge fused to certainty. They can become local antagonists who justify severe intervention in the name of preventing something worse. The most principled organization is exactly the one whose certainty can curdle.

Their answer to who decides what's real: I do — I decide who survives, for their own good.

External Powers Practical

The Rock of Bral, merchant powers, mercane traders, private captains, local governments, and world-level authorities all interact with the Fleet and the Order to varying degrees. Most do not understand the Prime Continuum in any meaningful sense. They still matter. They control lanes, supplies, politics, cover stories, contracts, rumors, and access.

Treat them as practical powers rather than cosmological experts. They are often the reason missions happen at all.

The disc-marked crate cache

At the Sky Dock. Where Vocath's grudge-loot pools — the visible thefts (Ryeback's pistol, restricted supplies, navigational charts). Cover for the quiet artifact-lift underneath. The crew found it and didn't open it.
